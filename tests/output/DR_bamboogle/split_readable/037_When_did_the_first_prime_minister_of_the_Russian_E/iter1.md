## Historical Background: The Creation of the Office

The roots of the modern prime ministerial role in Russia trace back to the aftermath of the Revolution of 1905, which brought widespread demands for governmental reform and greater representation. In response, Tsar Nicholas II issued the October Manifesto in October 1905, promising the creation of a parliament (the State Duma) and granting the monarch the authority to establish a cabinet responsible to the Tsar. This change marked the beginning of a constitutional monarchy in Russia and set the stage for the establishment of a modernized executive office.

By early 1906, the Fundamental Laws of the Russian Empire were amended to officially create the position of Chairman of the Council of Ministers, effectively making this post the equivalent of Prime Minister in Western states. The holder of this office would now serve as the principal advisor to the Tsar and lead the government cabinet.

## Sergei Yulievich Witte: The First Prime Minister

The first person to fill this newly established role was Sergei Yulievich Witte. Witte was a prominent statesman and reformer known for his efforts in modernizing Russia’s economy and infrastructure, especially during his tenure as Minister of Finance and Minister of Transport in the late nineteenth century. His reputation as an effective administrator and negotiator made him a natural choice for the first Prime Minister.

Sergei Witte officially assumed the role of Chairman of the Council of Ministers—equivalent to Prime Minister—on 23 March 1906. This date marks the official start of the prime ministerial office in the Russian Empire. His leadership lasted until 23 March 1909, providing continuity during a critical period of transition and reform.

## Context and Significance

It is important to distinguish between the traditional structure of the Russian Empire and the new system introduced in the early twentieth century. Before 1906, high-ranking officials such as ministers and chancellors operated within a system where ultimate executive authority remained with the Tsar. The introduction of the prime ministerial post represented a significant shift toward collective governance and accountability, influenced by developments in other European countries.

Witte’s tenure as the first prime minister was marked by ambitious policies aimed at expanding the railway network, promoting industrialization, reforming education, and attempting to improve relations between the government and emerging parliamentary factions. However, his time in office was also characterized by tension between autocratic traditions and calls for constitutional reform. These pressures eventually contributed to his resignation in 1909.
