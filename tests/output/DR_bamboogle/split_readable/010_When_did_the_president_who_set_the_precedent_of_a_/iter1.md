# Question

# Ground Truth Answer

# Model Prediction


The president who set the precedent of a two-term limit in the United States was George Washington, the nation's first president. He established this tradition by voluntarily stepping down after serving two terms, which created an important norm followed by subsequent presidents until it was formally enshrined in the U.S. Constitution via the 22nd Amendment in 1951.

George Washington assumed office and was inaugurated as president on April 30, 1789. His inauguration took place at Federal Hall in New York City. This event marked not only the beginning of the new federal government under the Constitution but also the establishment of numerous precedents that would guide the conduct and expectations of future American presidents.

Washington’s decision to serve only two terms reflected his belief in republican principles and his desire to avoid creating a monarchy or any form of political permanence in the executive office. By leaving office after eight years in power, he set a powerful example for future leaders. Subsequent presidents largely adhered to this precedent until Franklin D. Roosevelt broke the tradition during his unprecedented four terms in office, which ultimately led to the constitutional ratification of the two-term limit for presidential service.

In summary, George Washington entered office on April 30, 1789. His voluntary retirement after two terms set the standard for presidential tenure in the United States, providing a lasting precedent that helped shape the democratic traditions of the country.
