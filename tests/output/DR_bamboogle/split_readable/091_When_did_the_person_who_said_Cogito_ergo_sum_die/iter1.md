## Identification of the Philosopher

René Descartes (often anglicized as Rene Descartes) was born on March 31, 1596, in La Haye en Touraine, France. He became one of the central figures of 17th-century rationalism and is often referred to as the "Father of Modern Philosophy." His philosophical views and methodological skepticism fundamentally shaped subsequent thought in philosophy and the sciences.

## Key Contributions and Legacy

Descartes’ most significant contributions include:
- His formulation of the principle "Cogito, ergo sum," which serves as the foundation for his epistemology and theory of knowledge.
- Development of Cartesian geometry, linking algebra and geometry.
- Advancements in physics, particularly concerning the motion of bodies.
- An influence on many later philosophers, scientists, and theologians.

His major works include "Discourse on the Method," "Meditations on First Philosophy," and "Principles of Philosophy."

## Circumstances and Date of Death

In 1649, Queen Christina of Sweden invited Descartes to her court to instruct her in philosophy. Descartes accepted the position and relocated to Stockholm, Sweden, despite its harsh winters and dissimilar culture.

During his stay in Stockholm, Descartes faced considerable difficulties adapting to the local climate and lifestyle. Notably, the Queen preferred philosophical discussions in the early morning hours, requiring him to rise well before dawn—a practice that severely affected his health.

On February 11, 1650, René Descartes died in Stockholm at the age of 53. The official cause of death was pneumonia, attributed to the cold, damp climate and possibly exacerbated by the demanding schedule imposed by the Queen’s preferences.

## Historical Context

The early 17th century was a period of immense intellectual and scientific progress, commonly known as the Scientific Revolution. Descartes’ move to Sweden represented both an opportunity for intellectual exchange and a personal risk, given the stark contrast between the comforts of France and the rigorous conditions of Sweden. Despite the challenging environment, his time in Sweden was pivotal in disseminating his ideas to the Northern European intellectual community.

## Lasting Impact

Descartes' death marked the end of a prolific career during which he challenged prevailing scholastic traditions and laid the groundwork for modern analytic philosophy. His approach to doubt, reason, and certainty continues to influence contemporary debates in metaphysics, epistemology, and the philosophy of mind.

## Conclusion

René Descartes, the originator of the phrase "Cogito, ergo sum," died on February 11, 1650, in Stockholm, Sweden, following illness brought on partly by the environmental and social challenges of his residence at the Swedish court. His legacy as a philosopher and mathematician endures, making him one of the most important thinkers in history.
