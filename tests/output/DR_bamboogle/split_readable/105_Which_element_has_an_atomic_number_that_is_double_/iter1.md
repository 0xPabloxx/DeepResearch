# Question

Which element has an atomic number that is double that of hydrogen?

# Ground Truth Answer

Helium

# Model Prediction


Hydrogen is the first element in the periodic table and has an atomic number of 1. The atomic number represents the number of protons found in the nucleus of each atom of that element. When we double this atomic number—1 x 2—we arrive at 2. The element with an atomic number of 2 is helium, represented by the chemical symbol He.

Helium is a colorless, odorless, tasteless, non-toxic, chemically inert gas that belongs to Group 18 (noble gases) of the periodic table. It is the second-lightest element and only the second most abundant element in the universe, following hydrogen. While hydrogen is the lightest and most fundamental building block in cosmic chemistry, helium plays a crucial role in various natural processes and human applications due to its unique properties.

**Properties and Position in the Periodic Table**

Helium’s place in the periodic table is directly after hydrogen, reflecting its atomic structure and chemical behavior. The electrons in a helium atom fill the first energy shell completely with two electrons. This full valence shell imparts exceptional stability to helium atoms, making them highly unreactive. Unlike most elements, helium rarely forms compounds under normal conditions because it already possesses a stable electron configuration.

**Key Characteristics of Helium:**

- **Atomic Number:** 2
- **Symbol:** He
- **Group:** Noble Gases (Group 18)
- **State at Room Temperature:** Gas
- **Boiling Point:** Extremely low, -268.9°C (-452°F), making it useful for very cold scientific applications

**Significance in Nature and Technology**

In nature, helium primarily originates from the radioactive decay of elements such as uranium and thorium within the Earth’s crust, resulting in its release into the atmosphere over geological timescales. However, its abundance in the universe is much higher due to nuclear fusion reactions occurring in stars.

On Earth, helium is extracted commercially mainly from natural gas deposits, where concentrations can reach up to several percent. Its scarcity on our planet is due to its low density and inertness, which cause it to escape into space after being released from the atmosphere.

Technologically, helium is indispensable in various fields:

- **Cryogenics:** Used to cool superconducting magnets in MRI machines and particle accelerators, thanks to its extremely low boiling point.
- **Balloon and Blimp Industry:** Fills airships and party balloons because it is lighter than air and does not burn.
- **Leak Detection:** Due to its small atomic size and high diffusivity, it’s used to find tiny leaks in industrial systems.
- **Breathing Mixtures:** Used in deep-sea diving mixes to prevent nitrogen narcosis.
- **Semiconductor Manufacturing:** Provides an inert atmosphere during fabrication.

**Comparison Between Hydrogen and Helium**

| Element     | Atomic Number | Symbol | Group        | State at Room Temp. | Notable Properties                        |
|-------------|---------------|--------|--------------|---------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| Hydrogen    | 1             | H      | Nonmetal     | Gas                 | Lightest element, highly reactive         |
| Helium      | 2             | He     | Noble Gas    | Gas                 | Second lightest, extremely inert          |

Both elements are colorless, odorless, and highly stable under everyday conditions; however, their reactivity differs greatly. Hydrogen readily forms compounds, especially with oxygen and carbon, while helium’s complete electron shell makes it nearly entirely inert.

**Conclusion**

To directly answer the question: the element with an atomic number twice that of hydrogen is helium. Its atomic number of 2 marks it as one of the most stable elements in nature, playing key roles both cosmically and technologically due to its remarkable physical and chemical properties. Understanding this relationship highlights the significance of atomic numbers in organizing elements and predicting their behaviors.
