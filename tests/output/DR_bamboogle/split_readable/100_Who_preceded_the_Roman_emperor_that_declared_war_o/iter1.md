## Potential Historical Interpretations

### Military Campaigns Across the Sea

One way to interpret the phrase is by looking at emperors who led military expeditions requiring large-scale naval operations. Several notable campaigns fit this pattern:
- **Augustus (Octavian)**: After defeating Mark Antony at the Battle of Actium (31 BC), Augustus focused on eliminating piracy throughout the Mediterranean and securing Roman shipping lanes. This effort, known as the “war against the pirates,” involved battles and naval victories, securing the “Mare Nostrum” — “our sea.”
- **Claudius**: His invasion of Britain in AD 43 required a massive naval crossing, an event sometimes romanticized as conquest “of the sea.”
- **Justinian I** (Byzantine emperor): Launched the Vandalic and Gothic Wars against powerful maritime states, reasserting Roman presence in the Mediterranean.

None of these campaigns were publicly framed as a declaration against the sea itself. Instead, they addressed threats to Roman interests at sea — piracy, barbarian invasions, and rival powers.

### Symbolic Acts Against the Sea

A more figurative understanding arises from personal grand gestures or displays of power over the sea by individual emperors:
- **Caligula**: Ancient historians recount that Caligula allegedly ordered his soldiers to collect shells from the shoreline as “spoils of war,” treating the sea as if it were an enemy whose territory could be plundered. While this episode is widely regarded as satire rather than genuine policy, it embodies the notion of declaring war on the sea.
- **Naumachia Events**: Several emperors, including Caligula, Claudius, and Commodus, staged elaborate naumachiae — mock naval battles held in flooded arenas. These spectacles featured fleets attacking fictional enemies on the water, further blurring the line between war and performance.

This act is especially vivid in accounts by writers like Suetonius, who narrated Caligula’s preparations for “conquering” the Bay of Naples, treating the water as if it were enemy territory.

## The Predecessor: Tiberius

If we accept Caligula as the emperor who “declared war on the sea,” the answer to the question becomes straightforward. Caligula’s immediate predecessor as Roman emperor was **Tiberius**.

Upon his death, Caligula succeeded him and immediately set out to distinguish himself with increasingly erratic acts, including the dramatic spectacle involving the Bay of Naples.

## Broader Context and Additional Perspectives

### Other Possible Candidates

- **Augustus**, as noted, suppressed piracy in the Mediterranean. This created the illusion of a “war on the sea” for the sake of imperial security, though not in the sense implied by the question.
- **Claudius**’s British campaign certainly required crossing the English Channel, but it was not presented as a war against the sea itself.
- **Commodus** staged naumachiae, but these were performances rather than declarations of conflict.

None of these figures are as strongly associated with the concept of “declaring war on the sea” as Caligula, whose actions were so extreme and unique that they entered historical lore.

## Conclusion

**Tiberius** preceded Caligula, the Roman emperor historically noted for acts that could be interpreted as declaring war on the sea — most famously, ordering the construction of a floating bridge across the Bay of Naples as a grandiose assertion of control over the waters.

While the phrase “declared war on the sea” lacks a direct precedent in Roman history, it captures the imagination through anecdotes about imperial caprice and the boundaries between reality and spectacle. The closest real-world example comes from Caligula’s infamous attempts to dominate the sea, making his immediate predecessor Tiberius the correct answer to the question.</answer>
