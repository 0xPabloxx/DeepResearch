## Discovery of Superconductivity

Superconductivity was first observed by Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes on April 8, 1911. Conducting experiments at the Leiden Cryogenic Laboratory in the Netherlands, Onnes successfully liquefied helium and achieved extremely low temperatures near absolute zero. In his experiments with mercury, he noticed that its electrical resistance vanished completely at around 4.2 Kelvin. This unexpected finding contradicted previous understandings of conduction and introduced a new state of matter, fundamentally changing physics and materials science.

Onnes's achievement was the result of decades of scientific dedication and innovation in cryogenics. His work provided the foundation for further exploration into quantum mechanics and low-temperature physics, inspiring subsequent generations of scientists to investigate other materials exhibiting similar behaviors.

## Who Was the U.S. President in 1911?

In 1911, the President of the United States was William Howard Taft. He assumed office on March 4, 1909, following his victory over incumbent William Jennings Bryan in the previous year’s election. Taft served as the 27th president until March 4, 1913.

During his presidency, Taft focused on domestic reforms and progressive policies, including efforts to regulate large corporations and improve labor conditions. Internationally, his tenure witnessed diplomatic initiatives and the continuation of America’s emergence as a global power.

## Impact of Superconductivity

After its discovery, superconductivity spurred extensive investigation into new materials and phenomena. Scientists sought to identify other elements or compounds that could exhibit zero resistance at varying temperatures. Over time, breakthroughs led to higher critical temperature superconductors, which have found uses in medical imaging devices (MRI scanners), particle accelerators, and advanced electronics.

Additionally, superconductivity has implications for energy efficiency. Because superconducting wires transmit electricity without resistive losses, their implementation could drastically reduce energy waste in power grids, supporting sustainable development goals.
