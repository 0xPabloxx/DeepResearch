# Question

What rocket was used for the mission that landed the first humans on the moon?

# Ground Truth Answer

Saturn V

# Model Prediction


The mission that landed the first humans on the moon was Apollo 11, which marked a historic achievement in space exploration. The rocket used for this landmark mission was NASA’s Saturn V. 

The Saturn V was the United States’ super heavy-lift launch vehicle and remains the only rocket to have carried humans beyond low Earth orbit, specifically to the Moon. For Apollo 11, the rocket was designated SA-506. It stands out not only for its size and power but also for its reliability and engineering significance. The Saturn V was developed by NASA under the direction of Wernher von Braun and his team, and it represented a monumental leap in aerospace technology at the time.

The Saturn V rocket consisted of three main stages:

| Stage                | Description                                                                                  |
|----------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| First Stage (S-IC)   | Powered by five F-1 engines burning RP-1/LOX (refined kerosene and liquid oxygen); provided thrust for liftoff.|
| Second Stage (S-II)  | Utilized five J-2 engines burning liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen; continued acceleration after first stage separation.|
| Third Stage (S-IVB)  | Equipped with one J-2 engine; fired twice: once to achieve Earth orbit and again to escape Earth’s gravity and head to the Moon.|

This powerful vehicle enabled astronauts Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, Michael Collins, and thousands of ground support personnel to execute the complex maneuvers required to land on the lunar surface and return safely to Earth.

The Saturn V's role in Apollo 11 is fundamental not just technologically but also symbolically—it became the embodiment of human ambition and technical prowess during the Space Race era. Its successful flights made possible several subsequent crewed missions to the Moon, solidifying its legacy in the history of space exploration.
