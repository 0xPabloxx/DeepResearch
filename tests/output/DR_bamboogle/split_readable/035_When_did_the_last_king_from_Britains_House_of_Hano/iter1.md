### The End of the Male Line and Succession

William IV’s death on 20 June 1837 triggered a pivotal moment in British royal history. As he died without surviving legitimate children, the British crown passed to his niece, Victoria. However, Hanover had different succession laws; it required male-only inheritance, so Hanover passed instead to William’s brother, Ernest Augustus, Duke of Cumberland. This event marked the definitive end of the personal union between the British monarchy and the Kingdom of Hanover.

Queen Victoria thus became the first female ruler from the House of Hanover, but she did not reign as a king. Therefore, William IV remains the last king from Britain’s House of Hanover.

### Life and Death of William IV

Born in 1765, William IV spent much of his youth serving in the Royal Navy, earning the nickname “the Sailor King.” His reign was relatively short but significant for reforms such as the Great Reform Act of 1832, which expanded voting rights and addressed issues of representation in Parliament. 

William IV died at the age of 71, at Windsor Castle. His funeral was held several days later, and his legacy includes a period of constitutional and social change in Britain. The transition to Victoria’s rule marked the start of the Victorian era, one of the most transformative periods in British history.
