## Tycho Brahe: The Father of Observational Astronomy

Tycho Brahe, a Danish nobleman born in 1546, is widely recognized as a principal founder of systematic, high-precision astronomical observation. Living in an era before the invention of the telescope, Brahe designed and built large, state-of-the-art instruments to measure the positions of stars and planets with unprecedented accuracy. His observatories—most notably Uraniborg and Stjerneborg—served as centers for meticulous sky surveys and planetary tracking.

Brahe’s commitment to data collection resulted in catalogues of stellar positions that remained the most accurate of their time. His empirical approach provided critical evidence that challenged long-held geocentric models of the universe and laid the groundwork for future astronomers like Johannes Kepler, who derived the laws of planetary motion using Brahe’s extensive datasets.

## Galileo Galilei: The Father of Modern Observational Astronomy

Galileo Galilei, an Italian scientist and polymath (1564–1642), is celebrated as the “father of modern observational astronomy.” Unlike Brahe, Galileo applied the newly invented telescope to celestial observation, allowing him to collect data at scales previously unattainable. His telescopic discoveries included the moons orbiting Jupiter, craters and mountains on the Moon, phases of Venus, sunspots, and the Milky Way’s stellar composition. These findings fundamentally altered humanity’s understanding of the cosmos and promoted the use of instruments for empirical investigation.
