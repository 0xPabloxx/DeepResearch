## The Founder (Father) of Behaviorism

Behaviorism emerged as a major psychological movement in the early 20th century, rejecting introspection and mentalistic explanations for human behavior. Instead, it focused on observable behaviors and their relationship to environmental stimuli. The psychologist most frequently credited as the "father of behaviorism" is **John Broadus Watson**. Watson formally articulated the principles of behaviorism in his famous 1913 paper "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It." He argued that psychology should focus exclusively on measurable, observable behaviors rather than subjective experiences like thoughts and feelings. As such, he is commonly regarded as establishing behaviorism as a dominant school of thought in American psychology.

## John B. Watson’s Background

John B. Watson was born on January 9, 1878, in Travelers Rest, South Carolina. His upbringing took place in a relatively modest, rural setting, where his family played an important role in shaping his worldview. Watson came from a background typical of Southern America during that period—a family marked by hardship, religious devotion, and economic challenges.

### Family Structure

Watson was the son of:
- **John Thomas Watson**, his father, who worked as a farmer and served as a Methodist minister.
- His mother's name is often cited as Maggie Louisa Leishman Watson (though there are slight variations depending on the source).

Tragically, Watson’s mother died when he was still quite young, which profoundly affected his emotional development and possibly contributed to his later emphasis on objective measurement and behavioral conditioning over internal states.
