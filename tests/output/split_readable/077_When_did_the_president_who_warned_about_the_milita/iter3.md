# Question

When did the president who warned about the military industrial complex die?

# Ground Truth Answer

March 28, 1969

# Model Prediction


The president who famously warned about the “military‑industrial complex” was Dwight D. Eisenhower. In his Farewell Address on January 17, 1961, he cautioned the American people about the potential dangers posed by the growing collaboration and influence between the nation’s military establishments and its defense industry—a combination he feared could gain undue influence over national policy.

## Eisenhower's Warning

Dwight D. Eisenhower served as the 34th President of the United States from 1953 to 1961. During his presidency, he oversaw significant developments in both military strategy and technology, especially during the early years of the Cold War. In his final public speech as president, he expressed concern that the increasing entanglement of the armed forces and private defense contractors could result in excessive government spending, erosion of democratic processes, and threats to civil liberties. His precise wording has become iconic:

> “In the councils of government, we must guard against the acquisition of unwarranted influence, [by] the military-industrial complex. The consequence of an error in self-restraint of national security is ruinous not only upon military personnel but also upon civilians.”

This reflection anticipated many debates that would dominate American politics for decades to come, including discussions about defense spending, arms races, and the ethical responsibilities of both government officials and industry leaders.

## Date of Death

Dwight D. Eisenhower passed away on March 28, 1969. He was 78 years old at the time of his death. He suffered a series of heart attacks over several years, ultimately succumbing to complications related to coronary artery disease.

## Legacy and Impact

Eisenhower’s warning resonated with many Americans and has since become a staple reference in political discourse regarding military spending, national security, and the balance between public and private interests. His cautionary words are frequently cited when discussing contemporary issues such as privatization of military operations, lobbying efforts by defense contractors, and the risks associated with prolonged military engagements abroad.

Even after his death, Eisenhower’s insights continue to inform debates about how best to manage the relationship between civilian governance and military affairs, ensuring that national priorities remain aligned with the broader interests of the public.

In summary, the president who warned about the military-industrial complex was Dwight D. Eisenhower, and he died on March 28, 1969.
