# Question

When did the president who warned about the military industrial complex die?

# Ground Truth Answer

March 28, 1969

# Model Prediction


The president who famously warned about the military-industrial complex was Dwight D. Eisenhower, the 34th President of the United States. His warning became one of the most quoted parts of his Farewell Address, which he delivered on January 17, 1961. In that speech, Eisenhower cautioned Americans about the dangers posed by the increasing power and influence of the “military-industrial complex,” highlighting the potential risks of government-military cooperation growing beyond reasonable bounds and exerting undue influence over national policy.

Eisenhower was born on October 14, 1890, and served two terms as president from 1953 to 1961. Prior to his presidency, he was widely respected as Supreme Commander of the Allied Expeditionary Forces in Europe during World War II, serving as a symbol of leadership and stability. After leaving office, Eisenhower stayed out of the political limelight, focusing on personal interests and reflecting on the new world order shaped by the Cold War and advances in military technology.

On March 28, 1969—just under nine years after issuing his now-famous warning—Dwight D. Eisenhower passed away. He was 78 years old at the time of his death, and his passing marked the end of an era. Many considered him not only a key architect of mid-century American policy but also a leader whose prescient warnings continue to resonate today, especially in debates over the balance between national security and civil liberties.

Eisenhower’s legacy includes his stewardship during times of relative peace, his careful navigation of international crises such as the Korean War armistice and the early days of the Cold War, and his commitment to fiscal responsibility and anti-corruption measures within government. His concern about the military-industrial complex emerged from observing rapid technological developments and the increasing cost of maintaining American military superiority. He believed that unchecked growth in defense spending and the close ties between defense contractors and policymakers could threaten democratic processes and divert resources from more pressing domestic needs.

Since his death, historians, policymakers, and commentators have revisited Eisenhower’s remarks repeatedly, particularly during periods when questions of defense budgets, privatization of military functions, and conflicts of interest have come to the forefront. His warning remains a touchstone for arguments advocating transparency, accountability, and the careful management of national security expenditures.

In summary, the president who warned about the military-industrial complex was Dwight D. Eisenhower, who died on March 28, 1969. His timely caution has left a lasting impression on discussions about the relationship between government, industry, and public welfare.
