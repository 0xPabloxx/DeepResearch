# Question

# Ground Truth Answer

# Model Prediction


Superconductivity was discovered in 1911 by the Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes at Leiden University in the Netherlands. During this historic experiment, Onnes observed that mercury suddenly lost all electrical resistance when cooled to very low temperatures, specifically near absolute zero. This phenomenon marked the birth of superconductivity as a scientific field, leading to decades of subsequent research and many technological advances in physics and engineering.

At the time of this groundbreaking discovery, the president of the United States was William Howard Taft. Taft served as the 27th President of the United States from March 4, 1909, until March 4, 1913. Therefore, he was in office throughout the entirety of 1911, making him the sitting U.S. president when the properties of superconductivity were first observed.

William Howard Taft’s presidency was marked by significant domestic and international developments, including efforts related to antitrust regulation, involvement in Latin American affairs, and continued American progress in industrialization. Domestically, his term saw both progress and controversy, with an emphasis on legal reforms and economic policy.

The timeline between the discovery of superconductivity and Taft’s presidency aligns precisely, as the experimental confirmation occurred on April 8, 1911. At that moment, Taft was firmly in the second year of his presidential term. His administration was contemporaneous with multiple scientific advancements during the early 20th century—a period characterized by rapid innovation in physics and technology.

In summary, when superconductivity was discovered in 1911, the president of the United States was William Howard Taft. His term included the years both before and after the landmark experiment conducted by Kamerlingh Onnes, placing him at the center of an era defined by scientific breakthroughs and transformative social change.
