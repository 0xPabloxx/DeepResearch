**Discovery of Superconductivity**

The phenomenon of superconductivity was first observed by the Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes. In 1911, working at the University of Leiden in the Netherlands, Onnes successfully liquefied helium for the first time, thereby reaching temperatures very close to absolute zero (approximately 4.2 Kelvin). Using these ultra-low temperatures, he conducted experiments with mercury, discovering that its electrical resistance abruptly dropped to zero below this threshold. This extraordinary behavior, now known as superconductivity, marked a pivotal advance in physics and set the stage for decades of further research into quantum phenomena and materials science.

The specific date most commonly cited for the discovery is April 8, 1911. Onnes’ experiments revealed not only that resistance could vanish completely but also laid the groundwork for future discoveries such as the Meissner effect and the eventual formulation of BCS theory, which explained superconductivity on a microscopic level. His work earned him the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1913.

**William Howard Taft’s Presidency**

At the time of this landmark scientific achievement, William Howard Taft was serving as the 27th President of the United States. Taft assumed office on March 4, 1909, following his election victory over William Jennings Bryan. He held the presidency until March 4, 1913, when Woodrow Wilson succeeded him.

Taft’s tenure was characterized by several notable events:

- **Economic Policies:** Taft continued his predecessor Theodore Roosevelt’s progressive reforms, focusing on antitrust legislation, tariff reform, and conservation efforts. He oversaw significant changes in trade laws, such as the Payne–Aldrich Tariff Act, which attempted to moderate the high protective tariffs established in previous administrations.
- **Foreign Policy:** Taft pursued the “Dollar Diplomacy” strategy, seeking to expand American influence abroad by encouraging investment in foreign nations. This policy led to increased American business interests in Latin America and East Asia.
- **Domestic Affairs:** The period saw social and labor unrest, including the outbreak of the Great Coal Strike of 1902 (which occurred just before Taft’s presidency, but its effects lingered), and growing public debate about economic inequality and workers’ rights.
