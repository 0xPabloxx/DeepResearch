Wooden lattice towers differ from typical skyscrapers or CLT high-rises. A lattice tower is a framework where beams intersect in a crisscross pattern—often triangular—providing structural stability while minimizing material usage. Historically, such designs were widely adopted for telegraph lines, radio masts, and firewatch towers, particularly in regions with abundant timber resources.

While many broadcast towers globally are made of steel due to its superior tensile strength and durability, wooden lattice towers remained relevant throughout the 19th and early 20th centuries. Their decline coincided with the widespread availability of steel and the demands of higher frequencies, which required taller and sturdier supports.
