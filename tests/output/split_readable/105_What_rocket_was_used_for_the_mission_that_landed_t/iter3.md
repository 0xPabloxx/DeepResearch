The rocket that was used for the mission that landed the first humans on the Moon was the Saturn V. This iconic launch vehicle played a central role in humanity’s achievement of landing astronauts on the lunar surface for the first time during the Apollo 11 mission in July 1969.

The Saturn V was an engineering marvel of its era, developed by NASA under the direction of Wernher von Braun and his team at the Marshall Space Flight Center. It remains the tallest, heaviest, and most powerful rocket ever flown, and it was essential for sending astronauts from Earth orbit to the Moon.

### Key Features of the Saturn V

The Saturn V was a three-stage rocket, each stage designed for specific functions in propelling the Apollo spacecraft toward the Moon:

- **First Stage (S-IC)**: Powered by five F-1 engines burning kerosene and liquid oxygen (LOX), this stage generated over 7.5 million pounds of thrust at liftoff, capable of lifting the entire rocket off the launch pad.
- **Second Stage (S-II)**: Equipped with five J-2 engines that burned liquid hydrogen and LOX, this stage took over after the first stage separated, accelerating the spacecraft further into Earth orbit.
- **Third Stage (S-IVB)**: A single J-2 engine propelled the Apollo command/service module and lunar lander onto a trajectory towards the Moon. After reaching Earth orbit, the third stage reignited to send the craft toward the Moon.

Here's a summary table of Saturn V’s main stages and their propulsion systems:

|----------|-------------------|-------------|-------------------------|--------------------------------------|

| S-II     | 5                 | J-2         | Liquid H2/LOX           | Enter Earth orbit                      |
| S-IVB    | 1                 | J-2         | Liquid H2/LOX           | Lunar trajectory, trans-lunar injection|

### Apollo 11 Mission Overview

Apollo 11, launched on July 16, 1969, from Kennedy Space Center in Florida, was the fifth crewed mission in the Apollo program and the first to land humans on the Moon. The crew consisted of Commander Neil Armstrong, Command Module Pilot Michael Collins, and Lunar Module Pilot Edwin “Buzz” Aldrin.

The journey was made possible by the Saturn V's immense power, which delivered the Apollo spacecraft—command module Columbia and lunar module Eagle—into space with enough velocity to escape Earth’s gravity and travel to the Moon. After separating from the third stage, the Apollo spacecraft traveled approximately 240,000 miles to reach the Moon, where Aldrin and Armstrong descended to the lunar surface aboard the Eagle, while Collins remained in lunar orbit.

### Why the Saturn V Was Uniquely Suited

Prior to the development of the Saturn V, no other rocket had the necessary lift capacity to carry both a crewed spacecraft and a dedicated lunar lander to the Moon. Its design enabled the transportation of three astronauts plus their life-support and mission equipment, which far exceeded the capabilities of previous rockets such as the Saturn I or Saturn IB.

The Saturn V stood at about 363 feet tall—roughly the height of a 36-story building—and weighed more than 6.5 million pounds at liftoff, of which almost 90% was fuel. This remarkable scale was required to overcome the gravitational pull of Earth and achieve the velocities needed for lunar travel.

### Legacy of the Saturn V

Only five Saturn V rockets flew to the Moon after Apollo 11, but all successfully completed the ambitious goal set by President John F. Kennedy in 1961: landing a human on the Moon before the decade ended. The Saturn V remains the only launch vehicle to have carried humans beyond low Earth orbit, and it continues to be studied as one of the greatest achievements in aerospace engineering.

Despite numerous attempts and proposals for large-scale rockets in subsequent decades, none have matched the Saturn V in terms of payload capacity to lunar distances. Its influence persists in discussions about future lunar missions, including the proposed Artemis program, which aims to return humans to the Moon using newer technologies but still referencing the pioneering legacy of Saturn V.

In summary, the rocket used for the mission that landed the first humans on the Moon was the Saturn V, specifically designated SA-506 for the Apollo 11 mission. This launch vehicle symbolizes not only the technical ingenuity and ambition of the Apollo program, but also a defining moment in human exploration and achievement.
