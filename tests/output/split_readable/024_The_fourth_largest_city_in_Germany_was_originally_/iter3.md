The Ubii invited Roman support in their conflicts, leading to the eventual establishment of the Roman colony under the aforementioned name.

Over centuries, the settlement grew in importance. With its strategic location along the Rhine River, Cologne became a major hub for trade, administration, and military operations in Roman Germania. As Christianity began to spread through Europe, Cologne rose to prominence as an ecclesiastical center, home to one of the most famous cathedrals—Cologne Cathedral—which still stands as a testament to its historical grandeur.

The name “Cologne” became widely recognized in medieval times, especially after the settlement lost its formal Roman nomenclature and adapted local language forms. Even so, traces of its ancient past remain, with many historical buildings and archaeological sites still visible today.

In summary, the fourth largest city in Germany—Cologne—was originally called “Colonia Claudia Ara Agrippinensium” when it was founded by the Romans in the 1st century AD.
