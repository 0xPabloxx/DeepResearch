### Founding and Development of Tokyo

Tokyo originated as a small fishing village called Edo. Its transformation into a major metropolis began during the Muromachi period (1336–1573), but its rise to prominence is particularly marked by the construction of Edo Castle in 1457. This fortification laid the groundwork for the city’s growth, eventually becoming the de facto seat of power in Japan under the Tokugawa shogunate starting in 1603. During this era, Edo expanded rapidly, becoming the largest city in Japan by the early 17th century.

However, the pivotal moment for Tokyo's status as a national capital came with the Meiji Restoration in 1868. The restoration marked the end of feudal rule and the return of imperial authority to Kyoto. As part of this transition, Emperor Meiji relocated the capital from Kyoto to Edo, which was then renamed Tokyo, meaning "Eastern Capital." This renaming and relocation signaled both a new political era and the formal establishment of Tokyo as Japan’s capital city.

### Establishment Year: 1868

Therefore, the year Tokyo was officially established as the national capital of Japan is 1868. Prior to this, Edo existed as a significant urban center, but it did not serve as the sovereign nation’s capital until the events of the Meiji Restoration. The name change to Tokyo and the subsequent reorganization solidified the city’s role at the heart of Japanese governance and society.
