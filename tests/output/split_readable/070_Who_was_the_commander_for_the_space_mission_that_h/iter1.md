The first-ever spacewalk, or extravehicular activity (EVA), took place during the historic Voskhod 2 mission. This milestone event occurred on March 18, 1965, when Soviet cosmonaut Alexei Leonov left his spacecraft and floated in the vacuum of space for twelve minutes, making him the world’s first human to perform a spacewalk. However, the mission itself required a crew with complementary roles and responsibilities, including a commander who was responsible for piloting the spacecraft and ensuring the safety of his crewmate during this groundbreaking operation.

The commander of the Voskhod 2 mission was Pavel Romanovich Belyayev. Belyayev was already renowned within the Soviet space program before taking command of this mission. His background included experience as a fighter pilot and extensive training in aerospace engineering, making him well-suited to lead high-risk, high-stakes missions such as Voskhod 2.

Pavel Belyayev’s leadership during the mission was critical in navigating the numerous technical challenges that arose. While Alexei Leonov executed the spacewalk, Belyayev manned the controls of the Voskhod 2 capsule, monitoring systems and preparing for any contingencies. The success of the mission depended not only on Leonov’s courage to venture outside the spacecraft but also on Belyayev’s steady hand and quick thinking during the return-to-capsule phase, where unexpected complications nearly jeopardized both their lives.

However, his legacy is perhaps most closely tied to Voskhod 2 and his role in supporting Alexei Leonov’s pioneering spacewalk, which paved the way for future EVA operations in both the Soviet and international space programs.

In conclusion, the commander of the space mission that achieved the first spacewalk—the Voskhod 2 mission—was Pavel Romanovich Belyayev. His expertise and leadership were fundamental to the success of this landmark event in the history of human space exploration.
