## 1. Identifying the Founder of Geometry

The phrase "founder of geometry" can be interpreted in different ways, depending on historical perspectives:

- **Thales of Miletus**: Often considered one of the earliest Greek mathematicians, Thales introduced geometric principles to Greece from Egyptian influences. He is sometimes referred to as the "father of geometry" due to his pioneering work in deductive reasoning and practical geometry around the 6th century BCE.
- **Euclid**: More formally recognized as the "father of geometry," Euclid authored "Elements," a seminal text systematically laying out the foundational principles of geometry. His influence cemented his status as the definitive founder of geometry in mathematical history.

Despite some ambiguity, Euclid is widely acknowledged as the canonical "founder of geometry," especially in educational contexts.

## 2. City Where the Founder Lived

Historical records indicate that Euclid spent much of his career in Alexandria, Egypt. Alexandria was a vibrant center of learning and intellectual exchange during the Hellenistic era, particularly under the patronage of the Ptolemaic dynasty. It was home to the famed Library of Alexandria, which attracted scholars across various disciplines, including mathematics.

Euclid’s major contributions, especially "Elements," were composed and disseminated within this flourishing academic community. Therefore, the city where the founder of geometry (Euclid) lived is Alexandria.

## 3. Who Founded Alexandria?

Alexandria’s founding is well-documented in ancient history. The city was established by **Alexander the Great**, the renowned Macedonian king, in 331 BCE. After conquering Egypt, Alexander sought to create a new city at the strategic Mediterranean port that would blend Greek, Egyptian, and Near Eastern cultures and serve as a hub of trade, knowledge, and power.

Alexander's vision materialized through the rapid growth of Alexandria, which quickly became the most important cultural, scientific, and economic center of the ancient world. Under subsequent rulers, especially during the Ptolemaic period, the city flourished even further, attracting luminaries like Euclid, Archimedes, Eratosthenes, and others.
