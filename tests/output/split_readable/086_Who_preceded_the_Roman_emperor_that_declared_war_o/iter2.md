The Roman emperor who famously "declared war on the sea" is Caligula (Gaius Caesar Augustus Germanicus), one of the most notorious figures of ancient Rome due to his erratic and extravagant behavior during his reign from AD 37 to 41. His alleged campaign against the sea is preserved in historical sources, particularly in Suetonius’s “The Twelve Caesars,” where Caligula is depicted as ordering his soldiers to gather seashells along the beachfront and proclaiming it as the “spoils of war.” This act was widely regarded as a display of madness or megalomania, as the sea cannot realistically be considered an adversary in the traditional sense. Thus, Caligula became infamous for what is sometimes interpreted as a symbolic or mock declaration of war against the natural world.

**Background on Caligula’s Reign**

Caligula came to power following the death of his predecessor, Tiberius. His short tenure was marked by extravagance, cruelty, and bizarre actions that alienated both the Senate and the Roman people. Caligula’s reputation for eccentricity includes stories such as walking on stilts, appointing his horse Incitatus as consul, and supposedly planning to make his friends and subjects suffer grievous fates for amusement. The incident involving the sea fits into this pattern—his attempt to enforce a victory over the ocean and treat its natural treasures as loot is cited as evidence of his instability.

**The Anecdote of Declaring War on the Sea**

According to Suetonius, Caligula ordered his army to conduct a “campaign” against the sea, commanding them to seize and bring back the collected shells as trophies of battle. He then paraded these seashells through the streets of Rome, presenting them as the spoils of his conquest. This act wasn’t merely playful but also carried implications of political symbolism and divine authority, as emperors often celebrated military victories with triumphs and public celebrations. By treating the sea as an enemy, Caligula blurred the lines between reality and fiction, further distancing himself from rational governance.

**Historical Context of Naval Warfare and Rome**

During the imperial period, Rome engaged in numerous naval conflicts, especially to secure trade routes, combat piracy, and defend its coasts. However, declaring war on the sea itself was unprecedented and unique to Caligula’s reign. Unlike previous emperors who focused on land-based conquests or real naval battles against foreign powers, Caligula’s actions stood out for their theatricality and apparent disregard for conventional diplomacy or practicality.

**Predecessors of Caligula**

Before Caligula assumed control of the Roman Empire, the throne was held by Tiberius, who ruled from AD 14 to 37. Tiberius was the second Roman emperor, succeeding Augustus—the first emperor—and is remembered for consolidating imperial power, expanding territories, and eventually retreating to Capri, where he spent much of his later years away from Rome. Tiberius’s rule was initially stable but grew increasingly paranoid and reclusive toward the end, leading to a decline in his popularity and effectiveness as emperor.

Caligula succeeded Tiberius immediately upon his death in AD 37, marking a shift in imperial style and attitude. While Tiberius maintained a serious and formal approach to governance, Caligula’s antics signaled a departure from tradition and set a precedent for the unpredictable nature of some future emperors.
