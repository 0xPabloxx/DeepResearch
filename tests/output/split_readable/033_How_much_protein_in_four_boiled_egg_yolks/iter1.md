# Question

How much protein in four boiled egg yolks?

# Model Prediction


The protein content in four boiled egg yolks depends primarily on the size of the eggs and how the yolks are prepared. However, most nutrition references agree that, on average, one large egg yolk contains approximately 2.7 to 3.0 grams of protein. When boiled, the protein content remains essentially unchanged, although slight reductions in water content may cause a modest increase in concentration. Here’s a detailed breakdown:

## Protein Content in Egg Yolks

Egg yolks are a rich source of high-quality protein and essential nutrients. On average:
- **A single large egg yolk** weighs about 17 grams when raw. When boiled, some water is lost, slightly increasing the percentage of protein by weight.
- **Protein per yolk:** Each yolk provides roughly 2.7–3.0 grams of protein.
- **For four yolks:** This amounts to approximately 10.8–12.0 grams of protein in total.

Below is a markdown table summarizing the protein content for different scenarios:

| Preparation           | Yolk Size   | Protein per Yolk (g) | Protein in 4 Yolks (g) |
|-----------------------|-------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Raw                   | Large       | ~2.7                | ~10.8                 |
| Boiled (typical)      | Large       | ~2.8–3.0            | ~11.2–12.0            |
| Boiled (smaller yolk) | Small       | ~2.4–2.6            | ~9.6–10.4             |

This table shows that the exact amount will vary with egg size. For consistency, using large yolks as reference is standard in nutritional data.

## Factors Affecting Protein Content

Several factors can influence the protein measurement in boiled egg yolks:
- **Size of the egg:** Larger eggs produce larger yolks, which naturally contain more protein. Very small or extra-large yolks may differ slightly.
- **Cooking method:** Boiling preserves protein levels effectively. Slight variations in cooking time do not significantly alter protein content.
- **Moisture changes:** During boiling, water evaporates from the yolk, making the protein content proportionally higher than in the raw state.

However, these changes are relatively minor, and most references treat the protein content between raw and boiled eggs as equivalent for practical purposes.

## Protein Quality and Nutritional Profile

Egg yolks are not just high in protein; they also provide all nine essential amino acids required for human health, making them a complete protein source. In addition to protein, yolks contain significant amounts of vitamins (A, D, E, K, B12), minerals (iron, selenium, zinc), and healthy fats (including cholesterol and choline).

## Comparisons With Egg Whites

While yolks offer substantial protein, egg whites actually contain more protein per unit weight—about 10.9 grams per 100 grams, compared to about 16 grams per 100 grams in yolks. However, egg whites have fewer calories and almost no fat or cholesterol. Still, yolks contribute significantly to the daily protein intake, especially in dishes where both whites and yolks are consumed together.

## Practical Implications

If your goal is to track dietary protein, especially in meals centered around eggs (like breakfast omelets, deviled eggs, or egg salads), knowing the protein contribution from yolks is helpful. For instance, four boiled egg yolks will add roughly 11–12 grams of protein to your meal—important if you’re following a high-protein diet or looking to optimize nutrient balance.

## Summary

- **Average protein in four boiled egg yolks:** Approximately 11 grams (ranging from 9.6 to 12 grams depending on yolk size and preparation).
- **For large yolks:** Around 2.8–3.0 grams per yolk, totaling about 11–12 grams for four.
- **Nutritionally significant:** Egg yolks are a complete protein source, providing all essential amino acids, and add additional nutrients beyond protein.

Always keep in mind that precise measurements can vary slightly depending on egg size and whether the measurement is made before or after cooking. Nonetheless, the figures provided above represent widely accepted averages used in nutrition science.
