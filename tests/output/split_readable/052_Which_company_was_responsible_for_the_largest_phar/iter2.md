In 2012, GSK agreed to pay a historic sum of $3 billion to resolve both criminal and civil investigations conducted by the United States government. This landmark settlement addressed a wide array of allegations, including the illegal marketing of several medications for uses not approved by regulatory authorities, failure to report safety issues, and other misconduct related to drugs such as Paxil (an antidepressant) and Avandia (used to treat diabetes). At the time, this settlement set a new precedent as the largest ever paid by a pharmaceutical company for settling U.S. government investigations into pharmaceutical practices.

This settlement was notable not only for its financial magnitude but also for the breadth of its scope. GSK’s case involved criminal charges alongside civil False Claims Act violations, underscoring the seriousness with which these types of violations are treated under American law. The settlement funds were distributed among federal and state governments and marked a significant moment for accountability within the industry.

While GSK’s $3 billion payment represents the largest single pharmaceutical settlement for drug marketing violations up to this point, it is important to recognize the context of more recent litigation surrounding the opioid crisis. Several companies, notably Purdue Pharma (the maker of OxyContin) and Johnson & Johnson, have been subject to extensive lawsuits related to the national opioid epidemic. These cases often involve thousands of claimants and reach into the billions of dollars, sometimes surpassing GSK’s settlement in total aggregate value.

For example, Purdue Pharma entered bankruptcy proceedings in 2019, with proposed settlements reaching up to $7.4 billion—though much of this payment comes from private family assets rather than company profits. Johnson & Johnson, after facing a number of large jury awards (including a $5 billion punitive damages judgment), eventually settled certain cases for amounts exceeding $2 billion. However, these figures frequently encompass collective agreements with multiple plaintiffs or jurisdictions and may not always reflect direct payments solely from the company’s corporate assets.
