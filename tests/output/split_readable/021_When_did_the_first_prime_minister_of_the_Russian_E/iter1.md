## Historical Context: The Birth of the Prime Ministerial Office

Prior to 1905, the Russian Empire operated under an autocratic system where the Tsar wielded supreme authority, and no official prime minister existed. The title “prime minister” or “chairman of the council of ministers” only entered Russian governmental terminology as a consequence of the political reforms initiated after the Revolution of 1905. This upheaval forced Tsar Nicholas II to grant concessions to burgeoning demands for constitutional reform, resulting in the promulgation of the October Manifesto on October 17, 1905 (according to the Julian calendar, still in use in Russia at that time).

With these reforms came the establishment of the State Council and the introduction of the position of chairman of the council of ministers, which functioned as the de facto prime minister. The creation of this office represented a significant departure from centuries of centralized monarchical rule, introducing new layers of bureaucratic governance.

## Appointment of Sergei Witte

Sergei Witte, a prominent economist and long-serving Minister of Finance, was chosen by Tsar Nicholas II to lead the new cabinet. Witte was already well-known for his transformative economic policies, including his leadership in developing Russia’s railway network and industrialization efforts. His appointment was both symbolic and pragmatic, signaling the tsar’s intent to reform the government and address growing social unrest.

Count Witte officially assumed office on October 23, 1905, by the old (Julian) calendar used in Russia at that time. To convert this date to the modern (Gregorian) calendar, which is in widespread use internationally today, 13 days must be added—reflecting the difference between the two calendars in the 20th century. Thus, October 23, 1905, in the Julian calendar corresponds to November 5, 1905, in the Gregorian calendar.

## Role and Significance of the First Prime Minister

As prime minister, Witte’s responsibilities included chairing the newly formed Council of Ministers, coordinating government policy, and acting as the main intermediary between the Tsar and his ministers. Although his power was still limited by the continued supremacy of the monarch, Witte nonetheless had considerable influence over the administration’s day-to-day operations and the implementation of key reforms.

One of Witte’s immediate challenges was navigating the complex landscape of political demands and mitigating the effects of revolutionary agitation. He sought to enact measures aimed at increasing stability and improving social conditions, while simultaneously resisting radical calls for deeper constitutional change.

## Impact and Legacy

Witte’s premiership is remembered as a period of cautious reform and institutional transition. Though his term was relatively short, lasting just under seven months, he set precedents for subsequent governments and played a pivotal role in shaping the direction of Russian politics during a tumultuous era.

His administration laid the groundwork for further political evolution, even as the broader legacy of imperial reform remained contested. Many of Witte’s economic strategies continued to influence Russian policy in the years that followed, even after his resignation in May 1906.
