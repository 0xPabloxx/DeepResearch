Unlike many Western European monarchies, where constitutional structures had already incorporated a prime ministerial role long before, the Russian state was characterized by centralized autocratic rule under the tsar for centuries.

## Historical Background

The Russian Empire underwent several periods of reform and social upheaval before finally establishing a government structure that included a head of government responsible to a legislative body. Throughout much of its history, the Russian monarchy, under the leadership of the tsars, maintained supreme authority, and governance was conducted through various ministries without a single official serving as the chief executive or head of government in the modern sense. Instead, the tsar was both head of state and head of government.

Until the beginning of the twentieth century, important administrative roles were fulfilled by powerful ministers or secretaries, such as Prince Alexander Danilovich Menshikov during the reign of Peter the Great, but none held the formal title of prime minister. Even in times when influential courtiers wielded great power, the absence of a constitution meant that there was no legal framework to appoint or limit the powers of a prime minister.

## The Revolution of 1905 and Political Change

The push for a constitutional monarchy and greater governmental accountability gathered momentum during the Revolution of 1905. This period saw mass protests, strikes, and uprisings across the empire, culminating in the October Manifesto issued by Tsar Nicholas II. The manifesto promised basic civil liberties and the creation of an elected legislative assembly, the State Duma. In response, the emperor agreed to form a new government structure that would include a prime ministerial post.

On these grounds, the position of Chairman of the Council of Ministers was officially established, marking a significant step toward parliamentary governance in Russia. This position functioned similarly to a prime minister in other constitutional monarchies, acting as the chief executive officer of the state, presiding over the council, and coordinating policy between different ministries.

## Appointment of the First Prime Minister

Count Sergei Yulyevich Witte was chosen to become the first person to hold this newly created office. Witte had previously served as Finance Minister and was known for his ambitious plans to industrialize Russia and modernize its infrastructure. His experience and reputation made him a natural choice for the position of chairman tasked with managing the transition to constitutional rule.

Witte officially assumed his new duties on 2 November 1905 according to the Julian calendar, which was still in use in Russia at the time. When converted to the Gregorian calendar, commonly used internationally, this date corresponds to 15 November 1905. His appointment marked the beginning of a new era in Russian political history, even though the reforms would soon face resistance from within the tsarist regime.

## Role and Significance of the Chairman of the Council of Ministers

The appointment of Sergei Witte as the first chairman represented a shift in the nature of Russian governance. While the tsar retained ultimate authority, the presence of a prime minister allowed for more specialized administration and communication between the executive and legislative branches. Witte’s tenure focused on stabilizing the government, implementing economic reforms, and navigating the complex relationship between the monarchy and the new parliamentary institutions.

Despite his efforts, the position remained vulnerable to political instability. Witte was succeeded by other figures throughout the remaining years of the Russian Empire, each facing unique challenges, including World War I and rising demands for reform. However, it was Witte’s appointment that symbolized the empire’s move toward constitutionalism—even if ultimately unsuccessful in preventing the revolution of 1917.
