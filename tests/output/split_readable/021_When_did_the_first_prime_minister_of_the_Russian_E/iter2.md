## Historical Context

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Russia experienced increasing internal and external pressures, including social unrest, demands for political representation, and modernization efforts. These forces led to significant political changes following the Revolution of 1905, which forced Tsar Nicholas II to enact far-reaching reforms to prevent further instability.

Among these reforms was the introduction of the fundamental law “On the State Duma,” which established the legislative body known as the Duma. More importantly for your question, the Tsar issued the October Manifesto on October 17, 1905. This document promised civil liberties and laid the groundwork for a parliamentary system. Alongside the creation of the Duma, a new governmental structure was adopted to better manage Russia’s growing bureaucracy and emerging political expectations.

## Creation of the Prime Minister Position

Prior to 1905, the highest-ranking government official next to the Tsar was often referred to as the Minister-Chancellor or as the head of various ministries. However, these individuals did not act as a true prime minister, nor were they responsible for coordinating all aspects of the executive branch. The role was essentially fragmented among various ministers, with little central leadership.

After the reforms, a new position was created: Chairman of the Council of Ministers (equivalent to Prime Minister). The purpose of this post was to create a single leader who could coordinate policy, advise the Tsar, and oversee the functioning of the government in the wake of increased demands for responsiveness and efficiency.

## Count Sergei Yulyevich Witte: The First Prime Minister

Count Sergei Yulyevich Witte became the first person to hold the office of Prime Minister (Chairman of the Council of Ministers) in the Russian Empire. Witte was already a prominent statesman, having served as Minister of Finance and architect of major economic reforms. His reputation for pragmatic governance and his efforts to industrialize Russia made him a logical choice to take up the new premiership.

### Date of Office Inception

Witte officially came into office as the first Prime Minister of the Russian Empire on **23 November 1905**. This marked the beginning of a new era in Russian governance, aligning the empire more closely with the European tradition of strong centralized executive leadership.

Witte's appointment followed directly from the constitutional reforms associated with the October Manifesto. His responsibilities included coordinating cabinet-level ministers, presenting government policy, and acting as the main liaison between the Tsar and the newly empowered Duma.

## Significance and Legacy

Witte's tenure as Prime Minister was relatively short, lasting until May 1906, but his influence extended beyond his time in office. He navigated complex issues such as agricultural reform, railway expansion, foreign policy negotiations, and the fragile relationship between the autocracy and emergent liberal factions within Russian society.

His premiership represented the beginning of a more modernized Russian state, one that recognized the need for political innovation in response to both internal challenges and shifting international dynamics. The creation of the office itself was a concession to pressure for change and an acknowledgement that the Tsar alone could no longer respond effectively to the complexities of governing a vast, rapidly changing empire.

## Conclusion

The first Prime Minister of the Russian Empire, Count Sergei Yulyevich Witte, began his term on **23 November 1905**. His appointment marked a pivotal moment in Russian history, ushering in a new form of executive leadership designed to meet the challenges of modernization and political reform. This event was a direct result of the sweeping changes initiated after the Revolution of 1905 and remains a key turning point in the evolution of Russian political institutions.
