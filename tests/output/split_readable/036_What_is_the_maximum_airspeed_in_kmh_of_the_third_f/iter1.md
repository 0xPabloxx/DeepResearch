## Defining “Fastest Bird”

Birds have been ranked by their maximum achieved speeds in different contexts:

- **Stooping (Diving) Speed**: This refers to the incredible acceleration of certain birds when they dive from great heights to catch prey or travel quickly through the air. The peregrine falcon is famously recognized for this kind of speed.
- **Level Flight Speed**: This refers to the speed a bird achieves while flying horizontally or maintaining altitude without diving. Certain types of swifts excel in this category.

Most popular rankings incorporate both forms of flight, but it's crucial to understand which measurement method is being referenced, as this can alter the outcome significantly.

## Top Three Fastest Birds

Based on widely accepted records and scientific studies, the hierarchy of the fastest birds often appears as follows:

| Rank | Bird Species                | Mode      | Maximum Airspeed (km/h) |
|------|-----------------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| 1    | Peregrine Falcon            | Stooping  | ~389                   |
| 2    | Golden Eagle                | Stooping  | ~320                   |
| 3    | White-throated Needletail   | Level     | ~170                   |

### 1. Peregrine Falcon
This bird is renowned for its extraordinary stooping speed, reaching up to approximately 389 km/h. This speed is achieved as the bird dives from great heights, allowing gravity and aerodynamics to propel it to the highest recorded velocities among all living creatures.

### 2. Golden Eagle
The golden eagle ranks second, primarily due to its powerful dives. Recorded maximum speeds reach about 320 km/h during hunting maneuvers. Like the peregrine falcon, it uses a steep dive to maximize velocity.

### 3. White-throated Needletail (Spine-tailed Swift)
When discussing non-diving flight, the third fastest bird is usually the white-throated needletail, sometimes called the spine-tailed swift. It holds the record for level flight speed, capable of reaching up to about 170 km/h (or 105 mph). Unlike the peregrine falcon and golden eagle, the needletail’s speed is achieved during horizontal or migration flight, leveraging its streamlined body and long, swept wings to minimize drag and maximize thrust.

## Additional Context: Other High-Speed Birds

While the white-throated needletail generally takes third place, several other swifts and seabirds also demonstrate impressive speeds during regular flight:

- **Common Swift**: Known for sustained flight at speeds around 161 km/h.
- **Alpine Swift**: Also achieves speeds close to 150 km/h during migration or fast horizontal travel.

These birds are especially adapted for long periods of flight, often migrating thousands of kilometers with minimal stops, relying on their high-speed capabilities to cover vast distances efficiently.

## Why the Third Place Is Important

The white-throated needletail stands out because, aside from raptors engaged in rapid dives, it represents one of the very few birds that regularly attain such high speeds while flying level. This ability is critical for survival — not just for escaping predators or chasing prey, but for covering large territories and ensuring timely migration.

## Scientific Understanding of Swift Flight

Swifts, including the white-throated needletail, possess unique physical adaptations:

- **Wing Structure**: Long, pointed wings allow for faster and more efficient movement through the air.
- **Lightweight Bodies**: This reduces energy expenditure during extended flights.
- **Aerodynamic Shapes**: Their bodies minimize resistance even at high speeds.

These adaptations make them able to exploit wind currents and maintain velocity over long durations, distinguishing them from heavier-bodied birds who rely on bursts of power rather than sustained flight speed.

## Limitations in Measurements

It’s important to acknowledge that maximum speeds can vary depending on environmental conditions (such as wind speed and direction), the specific activities of the bird (e.g., hunting vs. migration), and differences in individual physiology. Most measurements are estimates based on tracking devices, high-speed photography, and direct observation.

Additionally, historical discrepancies exist between sources, as early records were less reliable, and modern technology continues to refine our understanding of bird flight capabilities.

## Conclusion

In summary, the maximum airspeed (in km/h) of the third fastest bird, considering both diving and level flight, is held by the white-throated needletail (spine-tailed swift), with a recorded speed of approximately **170 km/h**. This places it far ahead of most other species flying horizontally, though behind diving raptors like the peregrine falcon and golden eagle.

Understanding these rankings enriches our appreciation of avian biology and highlights the diverse strategies birds have evolved to master the skies.
