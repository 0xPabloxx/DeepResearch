## Understanding the American Flag in Maritime Context

Flying the American flag means that a ship is registered and licensed under the laws of the United States and must comply with U.S. maritime regulations, crewing requirements, and operational standards. The Jones Act, passed in 1920, restricts shipping between U.S. ports to vessels built in the United States, owned by U.S. citizens, and flagged under the U.S. This law has a significant impact on the cruise industry, especially for companies focused on cruises within U.S. waters.

The cruise line most often cited as the only one that flies the American flag is **American Cruise Lines (ACL)**. Founded in 1980 and headquartered in Fort Lauderdale, Florida, ACL specializes in coastal and river cruising primarily within the United States, including Alaska, the Mississippi River, and select Caribbean destinations.

The Jones Act, which mandates that vessels operating between U.S. ports must be American-built, owned, and flagged, creates both barriers and opportunities. Larger cruise companies typically avoid the complexities of U.S. regulation and Jones Act compliance for international voyages but may lease ships flagged in foreign nations for cost efficiency.
