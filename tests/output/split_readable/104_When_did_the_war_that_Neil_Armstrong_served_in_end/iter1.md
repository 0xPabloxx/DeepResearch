**Neil Armstrong’s Military Service**

Neil Armstrong joined the United States Navy shortly after finishing high school. At that time, he began formal pilot training and went on to serve as a naval aviator during the Korean War. His duties included flying combat missions over Korea aboard the aircraft carrier USS Essex. Although he left the Navy before the very end of hostilities, Armstrong participated directly in operations against North Korean and Chinese forces.

Armstrong’s service reflected the broader experience of many young Americans during the late 1940s and early 1950s. After completing flight training, he was quickly thrust into active duty, deploying to the Korean peninsula where the U.S. and its allies fought against communist North Korean and Chinese troops. His role involved tactical aviation operations that supported ground forces and enforced UN and U.S. objectives throughout the conflict.

**The Korean War and Its Timeline**

The Korean War began on June 25, 1950, when North Korean forces crossed the 38th parallel and invaded South Korea. This surprise attack triggered an immediate response from the United Nations, led by the United States. The ensuing conflict would drag on for more than three years and resulted in significant casualties on all sides.

Armstrong’s participation as a naval aviator overlapped with the later stages of the war. His deployment occurred after he completed flight training, and he flew a substantial number of combat missions—reports often cite around 78 missions—to support ground operations and contribute to air superiority efforts.

Despite his service continuing past the midpoint of the war, Armstrong left active duty in 1952, accepting a position to study engineering at Purdue University. Nevertheless, the conflict itself persisted for more than a year after his departure.

**The End of the Korean War**

The Korean War formally concluded on July 27, 1953, with the signing of the Korean Armistice Agreement. This agreement established a ceasefire along the 38th parallel, creating a Demilitarized Zone (DMZ) between North and South Korea. While the armistice halted major combat operations, no formal peace treaty was ever signed, leaving Korea technically still at war today.

Key milestones in the timeline are as follows:

| Event                         | Date           |
|-------------------------------|----------------|
| Korean War Begins             | June 25, 1950  |
| Armistice Signed              | July 27, 1953  |

Armistice negotiations had been protracted and fraught with difficulties, involving multiple rounds of talks over more than two years. The final agreement was reached only after significant battles and mounting casualties on all sides, including devastating effects on civilian populations and infrastructure across the peninsula.

**Context of the War’s Conclusion**

Although the signing of the armistice brought an official end to large-scale fighting, tensions between North and South Korea have remained high. The division imposed by the DMZ has contributed to a persistent state of caution and occasional skirmishes. For decades, the legacy of the Korean War shaped U.S. foreign policy in East Asia, influencing alliances, regional security arrangements, and the ongoing process of economic development for both Koreas.

**Armstrong’s Role in Historical Perspective**

While Armstrong himself departed the Navy before the armistice, his direct service took place during a pivotal phase of the war. The bravery and sacrifice of those who served, including pilots like Armstrong, became emblematic of American resolve during the era of Cold War confrontations. The technology and tactics developed and refined during this period laid important foundations for future military aviation, including the advancements that later propelled Armstrong onto the world stage as an astronaut.

**Summary**

To answer the specific question: the war that Neil Armstrong served in—the Korean War—ended on July 27, 1953, with the signing of the Korean Armistice Agreement. Though Armstrong left active duty prior to the formal conclusion, his contributions as a naval aviator were part of a larger national commitment that ultimately resulted in the cessation of hostilities and the establishment of the Korean DMZ.

The Korean War remains a defining episode in twentieth-century global history, highlighting the complexities of Cold War geopolitics and the enduring impact of international conflicts on individuals and societies.
