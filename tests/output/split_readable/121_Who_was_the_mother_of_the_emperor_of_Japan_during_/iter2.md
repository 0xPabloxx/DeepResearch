During World War I (1914–1918), the emperor of Japan was Emperor Taishō, whose personal name was Yoshihito. He ascended to the throne following the death of his father, Emperor Meiji, in July 1912. The period of World War I coincided with the early years of Taishō’s reign.

The mother of Emperor Taishō was Empress Shōken, also known by her birth name, Princess Haruko (春子), and later titled Empress Dowager Shōken after the death of her husband, Emperor Meiji. She played a significant role both within the imperial family and Japanese society during the transition between the Meiji era and the beginning of the Taishō era.

## Background and Role of Empress Shōken

Empress Shōken was born into the prestigious Katsura family as Haruko Katsura in 1849. She married Emperor Meiji in 1869, becoming his empress consort. As the daughter of Prince Katsura Aritsugu, she came from one of the most influential aristocratic families in Japan.

Her marriage to Emperor Meiji solidified her place at the heart of Japan’s imperial court. She was deeply involved in social, charitable, and cultural activities, championing causes such as women’s welfare, public health, and education. Even after Emperor Meiji’s death in 1912, she retained considerable prestige and influence as the Imperial Mother.

## Empress Shōken and Emperor Taishō

Empress Shōken was the mother of Emperor Taishō (Yoshihito), making her the first and principal female figure in the imperial family at the outbreak of World War I. She gave birth to Taishō in 1879, and he grew up under her guidance and care. Her relationship with her son was marked by mutual respect, although Taishō himself suffered from chronic health issues, which often affected the day-to-day operations of the imperial court.

While Emperor Taishō formally held supreme authority as emperor, much of the governing during his early years was managed by regents and his close advisors due to his poor health. Nevertheless, Empress Shōken remained a respected matriarchal presence, supporting her son and representing tradition and continuity in the imperial household.

## Empress Shōken’s Legacy

Beyond her role as mother to the emperor, Empress Shōken left a lasting legacy through her work in public welfare. She established various foundations, promoted medical advancement, and supported women’s education and rights. Her commitment to social progress made her a beloved figure throughout Japan.

After her death, she was honored as Empress Dowager Shōken and remembered as a symbol of compassion and national unity during a time of rapid modernization and change.

## Clarifying Possible Confusion

It is important to distinguish Empress Shōken from Empress Teimei, who was Emperor Taishō’s wife. Empress Teimei’s personal name was Sadako, and she became empress consort upon Taishō’s accession to the throne. Therefore, she was not his mother but his spouse, often confused due to their similar titles.
