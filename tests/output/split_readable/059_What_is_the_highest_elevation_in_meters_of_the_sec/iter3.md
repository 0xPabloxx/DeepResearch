# Model Prediction


The second largest island in the world is New Guinea, located in the southwestern Pacific Ocean. Spanning approximately 785,753 square kilometers, New Guinea is split between two countries: Indonesia occupies the western half (Papua and West Papua provinces), while Papua New Guinea governs the eastern half. Geographically, New Guinea is characterized by rugged mountain ranges, dense rainforests, and unique biodiversity.

**Highest Elevation on New Guinea**

The highest elevation on New Guinea is marked by Puncak Jaya, also known as Carstensz Pyramid. This remarkable peak is not only the highest point on New Guinea but also the tallest mountain in the Australasian region and the highest island peak globally. Puncak Jaya rises majestically within the Sudirman Range (also referred to as the Jayawijaya Mountains), situated in Indonesia’s Papua province.

The officially recognized elevation of Puncak Jaya is 4,884 meters (approximately 16,024 feet) above sea level. This measurement consistently appears in authoritative geographical records and scientific studies, reflecting its status as the island’s apex.

**Geological Context**

Puncak Jaya stands out due to its dramatic topography. Unlike most mountains, it features steep, glaciated faces, and it is composed primarily of limestone, which is unusual for such heights. Although glaciers once covered Puncak Jaya, climate change has led to their significant retreat over recent decades.

This mountain forms part of the greater Central Range running across New Guinea, a zone shaped by tectonic activity resulting from the collision of the Australian and Pacific Plates. The ongoing geological processes have produced the island’s extensive mountain systems and contributed to New Guinea’s reputation as one of the planet’s least explored and most biodiverse regions.

**Significance of New Guinea’s Topography**

The presence of such high peaks influences local climates, creating distinct ecological zones, from lowland tropical rainforests to alpine grasslands and glacial environments. These conditions foster extraordinary plant and animal life, including endemic species found nowhere else on Earth.

For mountaineers and scientists alike, Puncak Jaya remains a symbol of challenge and discovery. It is one of the famed “Seven Second Summits,” which represent the highest points of each continent except Antarctica. Conquering Puncak Jaya requires technical climbing skills, preparation for extreme weather, and respect for the remote, fragile environment of Papua.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the highest elevation of the second largest island in the world—New Guinea—is 4,884 meters, achieved by Puncak Jaya (Carstensz Pyramid). This peak represents both a geographic landmark and an icon of natural grandeur, underscoring New Guinea’s place among the world’s most significant islands in terms of geography, ecology, and human exploration.
