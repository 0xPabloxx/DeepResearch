## Background and Significance

The concept of a "fixed steel structure" refers to large-scale constructions primarily composed of steel, designed to remain permanently in place, rather than being movable or temporary. Such structures serve various functions, including telecommunications, tourism, and cultural iconography. Historically, significant feats in this category have included the Eiffel Tower, CN Tower, and most recently, the Tokyo Skytree.

The Tokyo Skytree stands at an impressive height of 634 meters (about 2,080 feet), making it the tallest structure in Japan and the tallest tower in the world. Its distinction as the tallest fixed steel structure is due to the predominant use of steel in its design and construction, particularly in the upper portions, while still incorporating concrete elements in the lower section for added stability and load-bearing capacity.

## Construction Timeline

Construction of the Tokyo Skytree began on December 17, 2008. Over the next several years, the project involved:

- Foundation work and excavation
- Construction of the reinforced concrete base
- Assembly of the steel framework for the tower’s upper sections

Completion of the structural work was finalized in late 2011. However, the official opening to the public occurred on May 22, 2012. While the top-out ceremony marking the highest point of the tower took place in December 2011, 2012 is recognized as the completion year for the entire project, including all final installations and operational readiness.

## Technical Features

The Tokyo Skytree incorporates advanced technologies and engineering solutions to address challenges such as wind resistance, earthquake safety, and durability. Key features include:

- A hybrid design combining reinforced concrete and steel
- A distinctive tapering shape intended to reduce wind load
- Sophisticated seismic isolation systems to withstand earthquakes
- Extensive use of steel for the antenna and upper support structures

These design decisions reflect international best practices in skyscraper and tower construction, especially given the seismic activity in Japan.
