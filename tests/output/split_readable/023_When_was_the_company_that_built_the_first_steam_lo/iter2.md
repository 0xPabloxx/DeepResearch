During the early 19th century, railways began to transform global commerce and travel. Steam locomotives quickly gained prominence as a means of mechanized transport. The first commercially viable steam railway to carry passengers over a public route was the Stockton and Darlington Railway in England, which opened on September 27, 1825. This railway introduced the era of regular steam-hauled passenger trains and marked a milestone in technological progress.

The primary locomotive for the inaugural journey was “Locomotion No. 1,” which hauled both goods and passengers, notably carrying hundreds of travelers during the opening day. Following close behind, the Liverpool and Manchester Railway (opened in 1830) further cemented steam locomotion’s role in passenger transport with advanced engines such as the “Rocket.” Both lines relied heavily on the expertise of one company: Robert Stephenson and Company.

Robert Stephenson and Company, based in Newcastle upon Tyne, was responsible for manufacturing Locomotion No. 1 and the Rocket. Founded by the renowned engineers George Stephenson and his son Robert, this firm was pivotal in revolutionizing rail transport worldwide.
