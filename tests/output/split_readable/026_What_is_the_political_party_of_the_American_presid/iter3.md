The Democratic Party has often advocated for increased government involvement in addressing climate change and promoting international cooperation on global challenges such as greenhouse gas emissions and sustainable development.

The Paris Agreement itself is a landmark international treaty adopted within the framework of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). Its main goal is to limit global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, while pursuing efforts to limit the temperature increase even further to 1.5 degrees Celsius. The United States’ entry into the agreement marked a significant shift in its approach to climate policy. Under Obama’s leadership, the U.S. committed to reducing national greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 26–28% by 2025 compared to 2005 levels. These commitments were formalized through the Clean Power Plan and other regulatory measures aimed at transitioning toward cleaner sources of energy.

In addition to Obama’s direct involvement, the broader historical context is important. Before the Paris Agreement, the United States had participated in earlier climate accords, such as the Kyoto Protocol. However, the U.S. never formally ratified Kyoto, due to domestic political opposition and concerns about economic impacts. The Obama administration viewed the Paris Agreement as a more inclusive and flexible mechanism for engaging all countries, including emerging economies like China and India, in collective climate action.

President Obama’s engagement with the Paris Agreement included diplomatic outreach with foreign leaders, negotiations in international forums, and efforts to build consensus both domestically and internationally. His willingness to commit the U.S. to meaningful emissions reductions reflected longstanding values of the Democratic Party regarding environmental stewardship and global leadership.

Following the end of Obama’s term, the United States briefly withdrew from the Paris Agreement under subsequent administrations. However, upon rejoining in February 2021, President Joe Biden—who is also a member of the Democratic Party—reaffirmed the country’s commitment to climate action. This rejoining process highlights the continuity in the Democratic Party’s approach to climate policy, emphasizing sustained advocacy for international agreements that address the global threat of climate change.

In summary, the American president who led the entry of the United States into the Paris Agreement was Barack Obama, who belongs to the Democratic Party. His leadership and the policies enacted under his administration were instrumental in establishing the U.S. as a key player in the fight against climate change. The Democratic Party’s enduring support for environmental protection and international cooperation played a fundamental role in both the original adoption of the Paris Agreement and ongoing efforts to fulfill its goals.
