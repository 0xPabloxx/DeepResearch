The concept known as the “hard problem of consciousness” is closely associated with Australian philosopher David Chalmers. This idea has become foundational within contemporary philosophy of mind and cognitive science, addressing the challenge of explaining subjective experience, or what it feels like to be conscious, as opposed to merely analyzing observable behaviors or neural processes.

David Chalmers introduced the term “hard problem of consciousness” in his influential work during the mid-1990s. His formulation sought to distinguish between so-called “easy problems”—those relating to functions, mechanisms, and information processing in the brain—and the “hard problem,” which concerns the deeper issue of why and how physical processes in the brain give rise to qualia, or subjective experiences. While easy problems are considered solvable through scientific investigation, the hard problem remains contentious and unresolved within philosophical circles.

Chalmers’ birth date is February 20, 1966. He was born in Adelaide, South Australia. Over the years, he has taught at several academic institutions, including Indiana University, the Australian National University, New York University, and most recently, Stony Brook University and the University of Arizona. His contributions span philosophy, cognitive science, and even aspects of artificial intelligence.

Chalmers’ articulation of the hard problem reshaped debates about consciousness, leading to new theoretical proposals such as property dualism and panpsychism. These alternatives seek to account for subjective experience without reducing it to purely physical phenomena, challenging conventional scientific paradigms.
