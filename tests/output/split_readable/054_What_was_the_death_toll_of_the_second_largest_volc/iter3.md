The second largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century was the 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines. This event stands as a critical reference point in modern volcanology due to its immense scale and far-reaching impacts, not just locally but globally.

## Identification of the Second Largest Eruption

Volcanologists typically rank eruptions according to their Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI) and the total volume of material ejected. In the 20th century, the largest recorded eruption was the 1912 eruption of Novarupta in Alaska, which released approximately 15–18 cubic kilometers of volcanic material. By comparison, the 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo expelled around 10 cubic kilometers of ash and rock, making it the second largest eruption of that century.

| Eruption                  | Year | Location             | Estimated Volume (km³) | VEI |
|---------------------------|------|----------------------|------------------------|-----|
| Novarupta                 | 1912 | Alaska, USA          | 15–18                  | 6   |
| Mount Pinatubo            | 1991 | Philippines          | ~10                    | 6   |
| Santa María               | 1902 | Guatemala            | ~7.5                   | 6   |

Other notable eruptions of the century, such as Santa María in Guatemala (1902), also reached VEI 6 but were smaller in volume than Pinatubo.

## Overview of the Mount Pinatubo Eruption

Mount Pinatubo’s eruption was preceded by several months of seismic activity, allowing scientists and authorities time to prepare for the catastrophic event. On June 15, 1991, the volcano erupted with tremendous force, sending ash and gases into the atmosphere hundreds of kilometers high.

This eruption resulted in:

- Massive ashfall covering surrounding areas,
- Lahars (volcanic mudflows) triggered by heavy rains mixing with ash,
- Displacement of thousands of local residents,
- Significant cooling effects worldwide due to sulfate aerosols injected into the stratosphere.

## The Death Toll

Despite its massive explosive power, the death toll from the eruption was not as high as might be expected from a catastrophe of this magnitude. Effective evacuation efforts led by scientists and government officials saved many lives. 

The direct fatalities from the immediate blast and pyroclastic flows numbered in the dozens. However, the aftermath saw additional casualties, particularly from lahars and health problems related to poor living conditions after displacement.

The overall number of deaths is generally estimated at between 800 and 1,000 people. Many sources specifically quote around 800 deaths, while others extend to nearly 1,000, accounting for both immediate and indirect causes such as disease, exposure, and infrastructure failures following the disaster.

### Summary Table: Mount Pinatubo Casualties

It’s important to note that these figures represent estimates, as reporting varies across historical records and local documentation. The complexity of tracking all causes—both immediate and delayed—contributes to the range given.

## Impact and Lessons Learned

Although the eruption destroyed towns, agriculture, and infrastructure, it also provided invaluable lessons in disaster preparedness and risk management. The successful evacuation of over 60,000 people highlights the effectiveness of scientific monitoring and public communication strategies.

Long-term, the eruption’s global climatic impact was notable. Ash and sulfur dioxide injected into the upper atmosphere led to measurable decreases in global temperatures over the following years.

## Conclusion

In summary, the second largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century—the 1991 eruption of Mount Pinatubo—resulted in approximately 800 to 1,000 deaths. Most of these losses occurred indirectly, as displaced populations faced health crises and environmental hazards in the months and years following the event. The eruption remains one of the most studied disasters in modern geological history, serving as a benchmark for understanding volcanic hazards and for improving community resilience in vulnerable regions.
