### Historical Background

The Song dynasty ruled China from 960 until 1279 CE. It was divided into the Northern Song (960–1127) and Southern Song (1127–1279) periods. During this era, China experienced significant advancements in culture, science, and economics, although it was often beset by military challenges, especially from northern nomadic peoples such as the Liao, Jin, and finally, the Mongols.

By the late 13th century, the Mongol Empire, under Genghis Khan’s successors, had expanded rapidly across Asia. Kublai Khan, the grandson of Genghis Khan, eventually unified the Mongol tribes and led invasions into southern China. After several decades of warfare, Kublai Khan succeeded in defeating the Southern Song dynasty in 1279, establishing the Yuan dynasty with himself as emperor.

### Succession and Final Emperor of the Yuan Dynasty

After the conquest and consolidation of power, the Yuan dynasty saw several emperors on the throne. The period leading up to its collapse was characterized by political instability, economic hardship, and widespread unrest. These factors culminated in the rise of rebellions that eventually forced the Yuan dynasty out of China proper.

The last emperor of the Yuan dynasty was Toghon Temür. He reigned from 1333 until 1368, the year when Ming forces captured the Yuan capital, Dadu (modern-day Beijing). Forced to retreat northward, Toghon Temür continued to claim the title of emperor among the remnants of the Mongol nobility living beyond the Great Wall, an era known as the Northern Yuan period. Toghon Temür died in exile in 1370, marking the definitive end of effective Yuan rule over China.

### End of the Yuan Dynasty

The fall of the Yuan dynasty in China was caused by multiple interconnected reasons:

1. **Political Instability:** Frequent changes in succession and court intrigues weakened central authority.
2. **Economic Troubles:** Heavy taxation, inflation, and corruption undermined public trust.
3. **Natural Disasters:** Floods, famines, and epidemics intensified suffering among the population.
4. **Popular Revolts:** Peasant uprisings and rebel movements, particularly those led by Zhu Yuanzhang (who would become the founder of the Ming dynasty), capitalized on the government’s inability to respond effectively.

In 1368, Zhu Yuanzhang captured Dadu and proclaimed the establishment of the Ming dynasty. With this victory, Toghon Temür retreated to Mongolia and continued to claim the Mandate of Heaven, but no longer held actual control over China proper. Despite lingering claims, the Ming dynasty officially succeeded the Yuan as the legitimate imperial power in China.

### Legacy
