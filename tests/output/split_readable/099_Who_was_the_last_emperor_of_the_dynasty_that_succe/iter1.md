Toghon Temür’s reign marks the end of both the Yuan dynasty and Mongol rule over all of China. He came to power during a time of significant challenges, including widespread social unrest, economic difficulties, and political instability. During his time as emperor, several peasant uprisings occurred due to heavy taxation, environmental disasters, and governmental corruption. These problems contributed to the decline and eventual collapse of the Yuan government.

When the Ming forces, led by Zhu Yuanzhang, approached and captured the Yuan capital at Dadu (modern-day Beijing) in 1368, Toghon Temür was forced to retreat northward with remnants of the Mongol court. Despite this defeat, he continued to claim legitimacy as the ruler of China from the northern steppes for a few years, leading to what is referred to as the Northern Yuan period. Toghon Temür died in 1370 in exile, marking the definitive end of Mongol domination over China.

To summarize, the key facts are:

- **Successor Dynasty:** The Yuan dynasty succeeded the Song dynasty in China.
- **Last Emperor:** Toghon Temür (Emperor Huizong of Yuan).
- **Reign:** 1333–1368.
- **End of Rule:** Expelled by the Ming dynasty in 1368, died in 1370.

This historical transition signified the close of nearly three centuries of Song rule and the rise of Mongol influence over Chinese governance, culture, and society. The end of the Yuan dynasty also paved the way for the Ming dynasty, which would go on to shape much of China’s history in the following centuries.
