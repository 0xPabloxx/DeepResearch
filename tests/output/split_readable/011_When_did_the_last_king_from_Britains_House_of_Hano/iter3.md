### Life and Reign

William IV was known as the “Sailor King,” owing to his service in the Royal Navy during his youth. Unlike some of his relatives, William was seen as sympathetic to the common people, reflecting a shift towards greater concern for public sentiment among the British elite. During his reign, several significant reforms were enacted, including the Reform Act of 1832, which expanded voting rights and restructured parliamentary representation.

Despite enjoying popularity among the public, William’s reign did not see the dramatic changes and upheavals experienced by those of George III or Victoria. However, his role as the last Hanoverian king is especially notable because it marked the end of direct male succession from the House of Hanover in Britain.

### Death and Succession

William IV died on June 20, 1837, at Kensington Palace in London. His passing brought about two momentous changes in British history:

1. **Transition to Female Monarchy:** Since William IV died without legitimate surviving children, the British crown passed to his niece, Victoria. She ascended the throne at age 18, marking the beginning of a new era in the monarchy.

2. **End of the Hanoverian Line in Britain:** With Victoria’s accession, the House of Hanover ceased to rule in the United Kingdom. Although Queen Victoria was a direct descendant of the Hanoverians, she carried the dynastic influence of her German ancestry, which would later lead to the royal family adopting the name Saxe-Coburg and Gotha under her son, Edward VII.

## The Broader Historical Context

While William IV was the last Hanoverian king of Britain, it is worth noting that the Hanoverian dynasty itself did not immediately cease to exist elsewhere. In the Kingdom of Hanover (located in what is now Germany), the throne passed to Ernest Augustus I, William IV’s younger brother, because Hanover operated under Salic law, which barred women from inheriting the throne. This separation lasted until 1866 when Hanover was annexed by Prussia and ceased to be a kingdom.

For the United Kingdom, however, William IV’s death ended not just his personal reign but the entire male succession line of the Hanover dynasty. After his death, the monarchy transitioned into the Victorian era and eventually shifted its dynastic name to reflect the new generation’s heritage.

## Legacy

William IV’s legacy includes modest reforms and his symbolic role as the bridge between eras. His reign laid groundwork for the more expansive changes introduced by Victoria, such as constitutional reform and the further modernization of the British state. His decision to support parliamentary reform and his popularity with ordinary citizens influenced future approaches to monarchy and governance.

Moreover, his passing led to the split between British and Hanoverian thrones. Whereas Britain moved firmly into a new phase of monarchical and political evolution, Hanover saw its own royal line continue briefly before being absorbed into Prussian territory.

## Conclusion

In summary, the last king from Britain’s House of Hanover was William IV, who died on June 20, 1837. His death marked the close of the Hanoverian era in Britain and the dawn of a new dynasty under his successor, Queen Victoria. William IV’s life and reign represent a critical link in the continuity of British monarchy, showing both the adaptability and endurance of the institution through periods of challenge and change.
