## Overview of the 1964 Alaska Earthquake

The 1964 Alaska earthquake, or the Great Alaska Earthquake, struck on March 27, 1964, at 5:36 p.m. local time. Its epicenter was located approximately 35 miles southeast of Seward, in Prince William Sound. This seismic event lasted for about 4.5 minutes and resulted in extensive ground displacement, landslides, and a devastating tsunami that affected coastal regions both in Alaska and as far away as Hawaii and California.

This earthquake stands out not only for its extreme magnitude but also for its profound impact on infrastructure, the landscape, and human life. It remains a benchmark for studying the hazards associated with major subduction zone earthquakes, providing valuable data for disaster preparedness and risk assessment.

## Fatalities and Casualties

The earthquake resulted in a total death toll of 131 people. The vast majority of these fatalities were caused by the resulting tsunami rather than direct effects of ground shaking. The breakdown of casualties is as follows:

| Cause                 | Approximate Number of Deaths |
|-----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Earthquake & aftershocks | ~7                         |
| Tsunami                | ~124                        |

### Breakdown

- **Tsunami**: The tsunami generated by the earthquake was particularly destructive. It reached heights up to 22 meters (about 72 feet) in certain locations, such as Crosby Cove in Alaska. Many communities situated along Alaska’s coast suffered severe loss of life as the waves inundated harbors, towns, and villages. The majority of the fatalities occurred in Alaskan coastal areas like the town of Kodiak, where the tsunami was especially deadly.
  
- **Direct Earthquake Damage**: A smaller number of deaths were directly attributed to building collapses and other immediate effects of the earthquake itself. These deaths occurred mostly in Anchorage, the state's largest city, where soil liquefaction led to widespread structural damage.

- **Secondary Effects**: Several additional deaths occurred due to indirect effects, such as injuries from falling debris, avalanches, and related accidents during rescue and evacuation efforts.

## Impact Beyond Alaska

While most fatalities were concentrated in Alaska, the tsunami traveled across the Pacific Ocean. There were also casualties reported in other locations:

- Hawaii lost 15 lives.
- Oregon and California each recorded several fatalities as well.

These further illustrate the far-reaching consequences of such an enormous earthquake and the interconnected nature of coastal hazards.

## Broader Consequences and Lessons Learned

The 1964 Alaska earthquake had lasting impacts beyond the immediate loss of life. Tens of thousands of people were left homeless, and billions of dollars worth of property and infrastructure were destroyed. Entire neighborhoods were altered by land subsidence and uplift, revealing new straits and mudflats where solid ground had previously existed.

This tragedy prompted a major overhaul in building codes, emergency management procedures, and tsunami warning systems across North America and the world. The event spurred research into seismic zones and remains a core case study for earthquake engineering, disaster response, and hazard mitigation efforts worldwide.

## Conclusion

In summary, the second most powerful earthquake ever recorded—the 1964 Alaska (Good Friday) earthquake—resulted in approximately 131 deaths, almost all in Alaska. The majority of fatalities were due to the tsunami triggered by the quake. The event served as a catalyst for improved understanding and preparation for seismic disasters, with lessons still relevant today.
