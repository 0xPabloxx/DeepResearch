## Overview of the 1964 Great Alaska Earthquake

The 1964 earthquake unfolded along a region that is part of the boundary between the North American and Pacific tectonic plates. The rupture extended over approximately 500 kilometers, resulting in extensive ground displacement and a series of cascading disasters such as landslides, subsidence, liquefaction, and devastating tsunamis. The destructive force of this earthquake was felt throughout southern Alaska and even impacted coastal areas thousands of kilometers away, notably in Hawaii and along the West Coast of the United States.

## Death Toll and Casualties

According to widely accepted historical records and reports from scientific organizations, the 1964 Great Alaska Earthquake resulted in approximately 131 fatalities. Most of these deaths were due to the tsunamis generated by the quake, which struck both Alaskan coastlines and distant shorelines across the Pacific Ocean. The breakdown of casualties includes:

- Direct fatalities caused by the earthquake itself, such as building collapses and injuries during ground shaking.
- Indirect fatalities attributable to tsunami waves that inundated communities after the earthquake. These waves reached heights of up to 67 meters in some locations and traveled across the Pacific, affecting Hawaii, California, Oregon, and Japan.

In addition to human losses, the earthquake caused severe economic damage and displaced numerous residents. Infrastructure, including roads, bridges, and buildings, was heavily compromised, leading to significant recovery efforts in the following months and years.
