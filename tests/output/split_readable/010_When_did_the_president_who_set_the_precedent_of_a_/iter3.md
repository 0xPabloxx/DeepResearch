# Model Prediction


The president who set the precedent of a two-term limit in the United States was George Washington. He established this tradition during his own presidency by voluntarily choosing not to seek a third term, thereby setting an important example for future presidents.

George Washington served as the first President of the United States and held office for two terms, from April 30, 1789, until March 4, 1797. His decision to step down after serving two terms was not required by any written law at the time, but rather was a matter of personal conviction and political principle. Washington’s choice reflected his belief that no individual should hold too much power over an extended period, and his action aimed to prevent the development of a monarchy-like government in the new nation.

Upon finishing his second term, Washington left office on March 4, 1797. This date marks the end of his presidency and the moment when John Adams assumed the presidency. During the transition, Washington delivered his farewell address to the American people on September 17, 1796, providing advice and warnings that remain influential in U.S. political culture.

Washington’s precedent of limiting himself to two terms had a profound effect on American political life. For nearly 150 years, every president followed this example, voluntarily retiring from office after serving two terms. This tradition persisted until Franklin D. Roosevelt was elected to an unprecedented four terms, breaking away from the two-term norm. In response, the 22nd Amendment to the Constitution was ratified in 1951, formally codifying the two-term limit for all future presidents and enshrining the practice established by Washington.

In summary, George Washington left office on March 4, 1797. His voluntary retirement after two terms set a crucial precedent in American history, shaping the structure of presidential leadership and helping to safeguard against the concentration of executive power.
