# Model Prediction


The United States presidential tradition of limiting service to two terms began with George Washington, the nation’s first president. Although there was never an official legal restriction on the number of terms a president could serve until the ratification of the 22nd Amendment in 1951, Washington himself chose to step down after serving two full terms, thereby establishing a powerful precedent that lasted nearly 150 years.

**Background of the Two-Term Precedent**

George Washington’s decision was both symbolic and influential. After leading the new nation through its formative years—including presiding over the Constitutional Convention, leading the Revolutionary War, and ensuring the smooth establishment of the federal government—Washington was unanimously elected as president in 1789. Despite widespread public support for him to remain in office indefinitely, Washington publicly declared his intention to retire after two terms. This act signaled to future presidents that voluntary retirement after two terms was honorable and consistent with republican ideals, helping to prevent the rise of a monarchy or executive overreach.

This practice was subsequently followed by most subsequent presidents, reinforcing the idea that no president should serve indefinitely. Only during Franklin D. Roosevelt’s unprecedented four terms beginning in 1933 did this tradition break, which eventually led Congress to formalize the two-term rule via the 22nd Amendment.

**Date George Washington Left Office**

George Washington formally completed his presidency and left office on **March 4, 1797**. His second inauguration occurred on March 4, 1793, and following two four-year terms, he handed over power to John Adams, his vice president and political successor, on the same date four years later. The choice of March 4 as the transition date was established by the original Constitution, although this was later changed to January 20th by the 20th Amendment in 1933.

**Why Washington’s Retirement Was Significant**

Washington’s departure after two terms was notable for several reasons:

- **Preventing Autocracy:** By stepping down voluntarily, Washington reassured Americans that the presidency was not a lifetime title and helped solidify democratic norms.
- **Setting a Tradition:** His action inspired other leaders, such as Thomas Jefferson and James Madison, to follow suit, contributing to over a century of stable leadership transitions without any president serving more than two terms.
- **Influence on Later Amendments:** The tradition laid out by Washington endured even though it was not codified into law. Eventually, after FDR broke the norm by winning four terms, Congress passed the 22nd Amendment, making the two-term limit constitutional.

**Transition of Power**

On March 4, 1797, John Adams was inaugurated as the second president of the United States, marking a peaceful transfer of power. This event underscored the success of the American experiment with republican principles and the importance of limiting executive authority.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the president who set the precedent for the two-term limit was George Washington, who left office on March 4, 1797. His voluntary retirement became a cornerstone of American political tradition, shaping expectations and practices regarding the duration of presidential service for generations. This precedent ultimately influenced the adoption of the 22nd Amendment, ensuring that no future president could serve more than two elected terms, except under exceptional circumstances.
