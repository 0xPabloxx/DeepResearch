The president who set the precedent of a two-term limit in the United States was George Washington. Understanding when and how this precedent was set helps illuminate its profound impact on American political history.

## George Washington’s Two Terms

George Washington became the first President of the United States under the new Constitution. He was inaugurated on April 30, 1789, and began his presidency with a clear sense of duty and humility. After successfully leading the nation through its formative years, he chose not to run for a third term. This decision was pivotal because it established a tradition that shaped the future leadership structure of the country.

Washington’s presidency consisted of two terms:
- **First Term:** April 30, 1789 – March 4, 1793
- **Second Term:** March 4, 1793 – March 4, 1797

On March 4, 1797, Washington’s presidency officially ended, and John Adams was inaugurated as the second President of the United States.

## Setting the Precedent

Washington’s choice to retire after two terms was motivated by several factors. He believed strongly in republican principles, particularly the idea that concentrating too much power in one individual’s hands could threaten liberty. By voluntarily leaving office, Washington demonstrated respect for democratic norms and the importance of peaceful transfer of power. This act helped prevent the emergence of lifelong rulers and supported the development of a stable, multi-party democracy.

After Washington’s example, nearly every president followed the two-term tradition. For over 140 years, the expectation was that presidents would serve only two terms unless extraordinary circumstances occurred, such as the death or resignation of an incumbent.

## The Evolution Toward Constitutional Reform

Despite the strength of the tradition, the precedent was broken by Franklin D. Roosevelt, who was elected to four terms between 1933 and 1945, serving through most of World War II and the Great Depression. Roosevelt’s extended tenure prompted Congress to propose the 22nd Amendment, ratified in 1951, which formally limited presidents to two terms in office. This constitutional change ensured that the practice begun by Washington would become permanent.

## Conclusion

George Washington set the enduring precedent of a two-term limit by voluntarily leaving office on March 4, 1797. His actions were crucial in shaping the traditions and expectations surrounding presidential power in the United States. The effect of his decision lasted well into the 20th century, culminating in the formalization of the two-term rule by constitutional amendment. Washington’s legacy is not only in the stability he brought to the government but also in his commitment to democratic principles, which continue to influence governance today.
