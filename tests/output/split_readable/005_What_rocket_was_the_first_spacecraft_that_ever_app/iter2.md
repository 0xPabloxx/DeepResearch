# Model Prediction


The first spacecraft to ever approach Uranus was Voyager 2, a robotic probe sent by NASA as part of its outer Solar System exploration program. Voyager 2 was launched on August 20, 1977, from Cape Canaveral, Florida. Its mission began the journey that would eventually make it the only spacecraft to conduct a close flyby of Uranus, which occurred on January 24, 1986.

### Launch Vehicle: Titan IIIE/Centaur

Voyager 2 was launched aboard a Titan IIIE rocket equipped with a Centaur upper stage. This launch vehicle was one of the most powerful rockets available at the time for planetary missions. The Titan IIIE was designed by the United States Air Force and featured a two-stage solid rocket booster configuration, while the Centaur upper stage provided the additional velocity needed to escape Earth’s gravitational pull and propel Voyager 2 onto its trajectory toward the outer planets.

#### Rocket Configuration

- **First Stage:** Titan IV E (Titan IIIE) first stage, powered by liquid oxygen and RP-1 kerosene, augmented with solid rocket boosters.
- **Second Stage:** Centaur upper stage, utilizing liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen as propellants, known for its efficiency in delivering payloads to high-energy trajectories.

This combination provided Voyager 2 with sufficient energy to reach Jupiter and Saturn on its way to Uranus and Neptune. The Titan IIIE/Centaur was a highly reliable vehicle for deep space missions during the late 1970s and early 1980s.

### Voyager 2 Mission Overview

Voyager 2 was the first and, to date, only spacecraft to visit Uranus. Its successful approach and flyby provided humanity with the first up-close observations of the planet and its system of rings and moons. The mission significantly expanded our scientific understanding of Uranus, including its atmospheric composition, magnetic field, and the characteristics of its satellites.

Key highlights of the Voyager 2 mission:

| Event                     | Date                 | Description                                             |
|---------------------------|----------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| Launch                    | August 20, 1977      | Departure from Earth aboard Titan IIIE/Centaur          |
| First Encounter: Jupiter  | March 5, 1979        | Closest approach to Jupiter                             |
| Second Encounter: Saturn  | August 26, 1981      | Closest approach to Saturn                              |
| Third Encounter: Uranus   | January 24, 1986     | Closest approach to Uranus (first-ever Uranus flyby)    |
| Fourth Encounter: Neptune | August 25, 1989      | Closest approach to Neptune                             |

Voyager 2 continues to operate today, still transmitting data from beyond the orbit of Pluto, making it one of the longest-running space missions in history.

### Significance of the Titan IIIE/Centaur Rocket

The Titan IIIE/Centaur was crucial for enabling interplanetary missions such as Voyager 2. The reliability and power of this launch vehicle allowed NASA to send probes not just to the outer planets but also to explore their moons and unique systems. The success of Voyager 2’s Uranus flyby relied entirely on the performance of the Titan IIIE/Centaur rocket, demonstrating the importance of robust launch vehicles for deep space exploration.
