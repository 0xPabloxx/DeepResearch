## Launch Vehicle Details

Voyager 2 was launched using a **Titan IIIE rocket**, which featured a Centaur upper stage. This launch vehicle was specially engineered to provide the necessary thrust and velocity for interplanetary missions beyond Earth orbit, especially for the ambitious grand tour of the outer planets undertaken by both Voyager spacecraft.

### Titan IIIE Rocket Configuration

- **First Stage:** The Titan IIIE’s core stage consisted of a two-stage solid-fuel rocket derived from the Titan III series. It had four strap-on solid rocket boosters to increase thrust during liftoff.
- **Second Stage:** After the solid rocket boosters burned out and separated, the core stage continued to propel the stack upward.
- **Centaur Upper Stage:** The Centaur stage was the most technologically advanced part of the Titan IIIE rocket. It used liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen as propellants, offering high efficiency and the ability to perform multiple engine burns necessary for injection into interplanetary trajectories.

This powerful combination enabled Voyager 2 to escape Earth’s gravity well at the velocity required to reach Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune, one after the other.

## Mission Context

Voyager 2 was launched on August 5, 1977, from Cape Canaveral, Florida. Just a few weeks later, its twin Voyager 1 was launched with the same ultimate destination. However, due to differing flight paths and planetary alignments, Voyager 2 became the only spacecraft to encounter Uranus.

The decision to send Voyager 2 through the outer planets system was based on a rare planetary alignment occurring every 176 years, which allowed for a “grand tour” of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune using gravitational assists to redirect and accelerate the spacecraft toward each subsequent planet.

## Technical Achievements

Launching a spacecraft with enough energy to reach Uranus required overcoming several challenges:

- **High Velocity Requirements:** Interplanetary missions to the outer solar system require high initial velocities, achievable only through robust launch vehicles.
- **Trajectory Precision:** The path of Voyager 2 had to be precisely calculated to use gravity assists from the giant planets, maximizing fuel efficiency and enabling multiple planetary encounters.
- **Reliability:** Given the vast distance and the impossibility of repair or maintenance once launched, reliability of both the spacecraft and the launch vehicle was critical.

The Titan IIIE/Centaur launch vehicle demonstrated proven reliability and performance throughout its career, launching numerous deep space missions including the Viking Mars landers and the Voyager pair.
