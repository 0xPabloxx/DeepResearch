**Launch Vehicle: Titan IIIE/Centaur**

The Titan IIIE consisted of a Titan booster (a two-stage rocket developed from military missile technology) augmented by solid rocket boosters for added thrust. After reaching a certain altitude, the Centaur upper stage—which utilized liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen as propellants—provided the additional velocity necessary to send Voyager 2 out of Earth's orbit and onto its interplanetary trajectory toward the outer solar system.

**Mission Context and Significance**

Voyager 2 was part of NASA’s ambitious Voyager program, designed to explore the gas giants of the solar system. Launched just five weeks before its twin, Voyager 1, Voyager 2 took advantage of a rare planetary alignment to use gravitational assists from each planet to propel itself outward. Its mission not only included the flybys of Jupiter and Saturn but also, uniquely, Uranus and Neptune—making it the only spacecraft to have visited all four giant planets.

The Titan IIIE/Centaur launch vehicle was essential to achieving these ambitious goals. Compared to other rockets available at the time, Titan IIIE/Centaur provided the reliability, thrust, and payload capacity needed to inject such a massive spacecraft as Voyager 2 directly into its multi-year interplanetary trajectory. 

The success of Voyager 2’s launch demonstrated the effectiveness of using combined booster and upper stage systems for deep space missions, influencing subsequent launches for probes like Galileo and Cassini. The journey of Voyager 2 culminated in historic flybys of Uranus and Neptune, delivering scientific data that revolutionized our understanding of these distant worlds’ atmospheres, magnetic fields, and moons.

**Key Details of the Launch**

- **Date of Launch:** August 20, 1977

- **Purpose:** Interplanetary exploration of outer planets  

**Why Titan IIIE/Centaur Was Chosen**

The Titan IIIE/Centaur was one of the most powerful American rockets of its era, capable of delivering payloads weighing over 800 kilograms to heliocentric escape trajectories. Its configuration made it ideal for missions targeting the outer planets, where even higher velocities than those achievable by lesser rockets were necessary. The Centaur upper stage’s cryogenic engines offered exceptional specific impulse, which translated into greater efficiency and range for interplanetary travel.

**Legacy and Impact**

The successful deployment of Voyager 2 by Titan IIIE/Centaur marked a high point in American rocketry and planetary science. Not only did the spacecraft provide unprecedented close-up views and measurements of Uranus, but its continued journey through the heliosphere has contributed to decades of scientific research. The rocket’s performance set benchmarks for future heavy-lift launch vehicles, showcasing the importance of advanced upper stages for deep space missions.
