However, in the late 19th century, a movement arose to restore Hebrew as a spoken language—a process closely associated with Zionism and the burgeoning desire for Jewish self-determination.

Eliezer Ben-Yehuda is universally acknowledged as the principal architect of this revival. He dedicated his life to transforming Hebrew from a written-only language into a living tongue used by everyday people in Palestine (then Ottoman-controlled), laying the groundwork for the Hebrew spoken today.

## Eliezer Ben-Yehuda: The Father of Modern Hebrew

Eliezer Ben-Yehuda was born in 1858 in Luzhky, then part of the Russian Empire (present-day Belarus). His birth name was Asher Ben-Yehuda, later changed to Eliezer, reflecting his deep commitment to Hebrew culture. Ben-Yehuda’s vision was radical: he sought to establish Hebrew as the national language of the Jewish people, enabling him to write children’s books, newspapers, and even invent new vocabulary for modern concepts.

His efforts included:

- Speaking only Hebrew at home with his wife and children
- Founding the Hebrew Language Committee
- Compiling the first modern Hebrew dictionary
- Publishing Hebrew periodicals

Through these endeavors, Ben-Yehuda succeeded in making Hebrew the official language of the Jewish community in Palestine and eventually the State of Israel.

## The Question of Family: Who Was Ben-Yehuda’s Father?

To answer who the “father of the father” is, we first identify the father of modern Hebrew—Eliezer Ben-Yehuda. Next, we focus on Ben-Yehuda’s father, whose identity is sometimes overlooked in broader discussions of Hebrew language history.

Historical records indicate that Eliezer Ben-Yehuda’s father was named **Yehuda Leib ben Haim** (often transliterated as Judah Leib son of Hayyim). This naming convention—“ben Haim”—follows Jewish tradition, where “ben” means “son of,” so Yehuda Leib ben Haim translates to “Judah Leib, son of Hayyim.” This indicates that Judah Leib’s father (i.e., Ben-Yehuda’s paternal grandfather) was Hayyim.

While specific biographical details about Yehuda Leib ben Haim are scarce compared to his renowned son, it is recorded that he was a Jewish merchant. Notably, in Jewish tradition, especially in Eastern Europe during the 19th century, family names often reflected the father’s given name rather than a hereditary surname. Ben-Yehuda’s surname itself comes from his father’s name: “Ben-Yehuda” means “son of Judah.”
